# Diseases of the Uveal Tract

The [[Uveal Tract]] consists of the [[Iris]], [[Ciliary Body]], and [[Choroid]]. Disorders of the uvea range from congenital anomalies to sight-threatening inflammations ([[Uveitis]]) and neoplasms.

### Classification of Uveitis
Before diving into specific diseases, understanding the anatomical classification is crucial for diagnosis.

| Type | Site of Inflammation | Primary Signs |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[[Anterior Uveitis]]** | [[Iris]] and pars plicata of [[Ciliary Body]] | [[Iritis]], [[Iridocyclitis]], [[Anterior Cyclitis]] |
| **[[Intermediate Uveitis]]** | Pars plana, peripheral [[Retina]] | [[Pars Planitis]], [[Posterior Cyclitis]], [[Hyalitis]] |
| **[[Posterior Uveitis]]** | [[Choroid]] and [[Retina]] | [[Choroiditis]], [[Chorioretinitis]], [[Retinochoroiditis]] |
| **[[Panuveitis]]** | Entire uveal tract | Generalized inflammation |

### Comprehensive Table of Uveal Tract Disorders

| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[[Congenital Coloboma]]** | Keyhole-shaped defect in iris/choroid/ciliary body (usually inferonasal). > [!warning] Diagram Alert
> Keyhole shaped pupil defect | Failure of closure of the **embryonic fissure** during development. | **Iris:** Cosmetic contact lenses/sunglasses.<br>**Choroid:** No treatment; monitor for [[Retinal Detachment]]. |

| **[[Heterochromia Iridis]]** | Difference in color between two irides or parts of the same iris. | **Congenital:** Horner's syndrome, Waardenburg syndrome.<br>**Acquired:** [[Fuchs' Heterochromic Iridocyclitis]], Siderosis, Melanoma. | Treat underlying cause. Cosmetic contacts if congenital. |
| **[[Persistent Pupillary Membrane]]** | Fine strands of tissue crossing the pupil originating from the collarette. | Incomplete atrophy of the **tunica vasculosa lentis**. | Usually none required. Nd:YAG laser membranectomy if vision affected. |
| **[[Anterior Uveitis]] (Acute)** | **Symptoms:** Pain, photophobia, redness, lacrimation.<br>**Signs:** Ciliary congestion, [[Keratic Precipitates]] (KPs), Aqueous flare/cells, Miosis, [[Posterior Synechiae]]. | **Non-Granulomatous:** HLA-B27 (Ankylosing spondylitis), Idiopathic, Trauma.<br>**Granulomatous:** TB, Sarcoidosis, Syphilis. | **Topical Steroids:** (e.g., [[Prednisolone Acetate]]) to reduce inflammation.<br>**Cycloplegics:** ([[Atropine]], [[Homatropine]]) to relieve pain and prevent synechiae. |
| **[[Intermediate Uveitis]] (Pars Planitis)** | **Symptoms:** Floaters, blurred vision (painless).<br>**Signs:** **Snowballs** (vitreous exudates), **Snowbanking** (inferior pars plana exudates). | Idiopathic (most common), Multiple Sclerosis, Sarcoidosis, Lyme disease. | **Periocular Steroids:** (Posterior Subtenon Triamcinolone).<br>**Systemic Steroids/Immunosuppressives.**<br>**Cryotherapy** or Laser to vascularized banks. |
| **[[Posterior Uveitis]]** | **Symptoms:** Floaters, decreased vision (painless).<br>**Signs:** Vitritis, Choroidal/Retinal patches (active: yellow/creamy; healed: pigmented/atrophic). | **Infectious:** [[Toxoplasmosis]] (Headlight in fog), [[Tuberculosis]], [[Syphilis]], CMV.<br>**Non-infectious:** Sarcoidosis. | Treat specific cause (e.g., Pyrimethamine for Toxoplasma, ATT for TB).<br>Systemic Steroids (under antimicrobial cover). |
| **[[Endophthalmitis]]** | Severe pain, vision loss, lid edema, hypopyon, vitritis. **Loss of Red Reflex.** | **Exogenous:** Post-operative (Staph epidermidis/aureus), Post-traumatic.<br>**Endogenous:** Septicemia. | **Medical Emergency:**<br>1. Intravitreal Antibiotics ([[Vancomycin]] + [[Ceftazidime]]).<br>2. [[Pars Plana Vitrectomy]] (PPV).<br>3. Systemic Antibiotics. |
| **[[Panophthalmitis]]** | Inflammation of all coats of eye + **Tenon's capsule**. Painful ocular movements, proptosis. | Spread of purulent [[Endophthalmitis]] to orbital structures. | **Evisceration** (Removal of intraocular contents leaving sclera). **Enucleation is contraindicated** (risk of meningitis). |
| **[[Sympathetic Ophthalmitis]]** | Bilateral granulomatous panuveitis after penetrating trauma to one eye (**Exciting eye**). Fellow eye (**Sympathizing eye**) affected. | Autoimmune reaction to uveal pigment/antigen. **Dalen-Fuchs nodules** (epithelioid cells between RPE and Bruch's). | **Prevention:** Enucleation of injured eye within 2 weeks.<br>**Treatment:** High dose Systemic Steroids, Immunosuppressives. |
| **[[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada Syndrome]] (VKH)** | Bilateral granulomatous panuveitis with **Poliosis, Vitiligo, Alopecia**, CNS signs (meningismus), Auditory signs (tinnitus). | Autoimmune reaction against melanocytes. HLA-DR4 association. | High dose Systemic Steroids (long term taper). Immunosuppressives for recurrent cases. |
| **[[Behcet's Disease]]** | Recurrent acute iridocyclitis with **Hypopyon** (mobile). Oral/Genital ulcers. Skin lesions. | Systemic vasculitis. HLA-B51 association. | Systemic Steroids and Immunosuppressives (e.g., Azathioprine, Cyclosporine). |
| **[[Fuchs' Heterochromic Iridocyclitis]]** | Unilateral mild blurring. **Heterochromia** (lighter iris). Diffuse stellate KPs. No synechiae. | Chronic low-grade non-granulomatous inflammation. | **Do NOT treat inflammation** (steroids ineffective). Treat complications: Cataract surgery, Glaucoma management. |
| **[[Posner-Schlossman Syndrome]]** | **Glaucomatocyclitic crisis.** Unilateral recurrent mild anterior uveitis with markedly raised IOP. | Idiopathic. | Topical Steroids (short course) + Anti-glaucoma meds (e.g., [[Timolol]], [[Acetazolamide]]). Avoid Miotics/Prostaglandins. |
| **[[Choroidal Melanoma]]** | Elevated pigmented mass (mushroom/collar-stud shape). Retinal detachment. | Malignant tumor of melanocytes. Most common primary intraocular malignancy in adults. | **Small:** Brachytherapy, TTT.<br>**Large:** Enucleation.<br>**Metastasis:** Palliative care (Liver is common site). |
| **[[Iris Melanoma]]** | Pigmented nodule on iris, distortion of pupil, secondary glaucoma. | Malignant tumor of iris melanocytes. Better prognosis than ciliary/choroid melanoma. | Iridectomy / Iridocyclectomy. Enucleation if diffuse or extensive glaucoma. |

### Key Differentiating Features: Granulomatous vs Non-Granulomatous Uveitis

| Feature | Granulomatous Uveitis | Non-Granulomatous Uveitis |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Onset** | Insidious | Acute |
| **Pain/Photophobia** | Minimal | Marked |
| **Keratic Precipitates (KPs)** | **Mutton Fat KPs** (Large, greasy, macrophages/epithelioid cells) | **Fine KPs** (Small, lymphocytes) |
| **Iris Nodules** | Present ([[Koeppe Nodules]] at border, [[Busacca Nodules]] at stroma) | Absent |
| **Posterior Synechiae** | Thick and broad-based | Thin and tenuous |
| **Common Causes** | TB, Sarcoidosis, Syphilis, Sympathetic Ophthalmitis | HLA-B27, Idiopathic, Trauma |

### Important Clinical Signs & Associations

*   **[[Hypopyon]] (Sterile):** Seen in [[Behcet's Disease]] (mobile hypopyon) and HLA-B27 Uveitis.
*   **[[Hypopyon]] (Infective):** Seen in [[Endophthalmitis]] and corneal ulcers (fungal/pneumococcal).
*   **Headlight in Fog Appearance:** Classic sign of active [[Toxoplasmosis]] retinochoroiditis (focal necrotizing retinitis with vitritis).
*   **Candle Wax Drippings:** Periphlebitis seen in [[Sarcoidosis]].
*   **Iris Pearls:** Seen in Lepromatous [[Leprosy]].
*   **Salt and Pepper Fundus:** Seen in Congenital [[Syphilis]] (and Rubella).
*   **Flower Petal Appearance:** Cystoid Macular Edema (CME) on FFA (common complication of uveitis).
