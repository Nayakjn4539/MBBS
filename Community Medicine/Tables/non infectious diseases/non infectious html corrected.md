| Coronary heart disease | Angina pectoris, myocardial infarction (MI), silent ischemia, heart failure, sudden cardiac death. | Host: Increasing age, male sex (higher risk until menopause), Type 'A' personality. Agent: Excessive saturated fats, sucrose, cholesterol. Environment: Stressful urban living, sedentary occupations (e.g., bus drivers vs conductors). | Behavioral: Smoking, high fat/sugar diet, physical inactivity, alcohol ( >75 g/day), psychological stress. Biological: Hypertension, diabetes, obesity, hypercholesterolemia ( LDL/HDL ratio). | Increasing age, male sex, family history of premature IHD, genetic constitution for lipid handling. | Symptoms: Severe chest pain/pressure, breathlessness, nausea, sweating, cold sweat. Signs: ECG changes, irregular heart rhythm, cardiac arrest. | Clinical presentation, ECG (ST-segment changes), serum lipid levels ( LDL , HDL , total cholesterol), cardiac enzymes (Troponin), coronary angiography. | Medical: Morphine, pethidine, beta-blockers, aspirin, ACE inhibitors, statins, oxygen. Surgical: CABG, PTCA (angioplasty), stent placement, pacemakers. | Health promotion: Prudent diet ( 2500-2800 kcal/day, fat 15-30% , saturated fat <10% , salt <5 g), smoking cessation, aerobic exercise ( 30 mins/day), stress management. | Early diagnosis through periodic check-ups after age 40, lipid screening, blood pressure monitoring, and use of 75 mg aspirin for recurrence risk. |
| Hypertension | Primary (Essential) (95%), Secondary (renal/endocrine), Gestational, Preeclampsia, Malignant, and Resistant hypertension. | Host: Age ( >40 ), sex ( M:F ≈ 2:1 to 3:1 in India), Type A personality. Agent: High salt, alcohol, caffeine. Environment: Stressful urban social environment, noise, soft water areas. | Behavioral: Salt intake ( >6-10 g NaCl /day), obesity ( BMI > 25 ), alcohol, tobacco, physical inactivity, mental stress. Biological: High sodium/low potassium ratio. | Age, genetic inheritance (polygenic), ethnicity (higher in black communities). | Often asymptomatic ('silent killer'), headache, giddiness, fainting, palpitations, or signs of organ damage (retinopathy, renal impairment). | JNC 7/AHA Criteria: Normal ( <120/80 ), Stage I ( 130-159/80-99 ), Stage II ( ≥ 160/100 mmHg). Measured by sphygmomanometer after 5 mins rest. | Antihypertensive drugs (thiazides, amlodipine, ACE inhibitors), weight reduction, DASH diet, salt restriction, renal sympathetic-nerve ablation. | Primordial: Discouraging harmful lifestyles in children. Primary: Weight reduction, salt intake <100 mmol/day, aerobic exercise, limiting alcohol, yoga. | Early detection through screening (ASHA/ANM), home blood pressure monitoring (ABPM), and ensuring treatment compliance to prevent stroke/MI. |
| Stroke | Ischemic (85%) (Lacunar, Carotid), Hemorrhagic (Intracerebral, Subarachnoid), and Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA). | Host: Increasing age, history of CVD/Hypertension. Environment: Household air pollution (solid fuels), modern lifestyle stressors. | Behavioral: Hypertension (primary risk), smoking, physical inactivity, unhealthy diet, alcohol abuse. Biological: Diabetes, obesity, high cholesterol, high CRP levels. | Age (significant increase in 70+ ), male sex, prior history of TIA, family history. | Sudden neurological impairment, hemiplegia/hemiparesis, facial drooping, aphasia (speech difficulty), confusion, vision loss, dizziness. | Clinical signs (FAST: Face, Arms, Speech, Time), CT scan or MRI to distinguish type, WHO STEPS surveillance. | Acute: Thrombolytics ( rTPA for ischemic within 4.5 hours), management of intracranial pressure, stabilization. Chronic: Rehabilitation services. | Controlling blood pressure (6 mm reduction reduces risk by 40% ), smoking cessation, clean household energy, healthy diet. | Early detection of TIAs, use of aspirin ( 75 mg), regular monitoring of BP and glucose, and disability limitation through physiotherapy. |
| Rheumatic heart disease | Acute Rheumatic Fever (ARF), Chronic RHD (Mitral/Aortic stenosis or regurgitation), Juvenile Mitral Stenosis. | Host: Children 5–15 years, inherent susceptibility. Agent: Group A beta-hemolytic Streptococcus (e.g., M type 5). Environment: Poverty, overcrowding, poor housing. | Untreated streptococcal sore throat (pharyngitis), poor personal hygiene, living in overcrowded conditions, lack of access to medical care. | Inherent genetic susceptibility (linked to non-O blood groups), age (peak 5–15 years), female sex (worse prognosis). | Carditis, polyarthritis (migratory joint pain), chorea (jerky movements), erythema marginatum, subcutaneous nodules, heart murmurs, ascites. | Revised Jones Criteria: 2 major OR 1 major + 2 minor manifestations plus evidence of preceding streptococcal infection (ASO titre, throat culture). | Acute phase: Intramuscular Benzathine benzylpenicillin ( 600,000 to 1.2 million IU), salicylates (anti-inflammatory), valve repair or replacement. | Adequate antibiotic therapy of group A streptococcal sore throats, improvement of housing, Jai Vigyan mission for vaccine development. | Secondary prophylaxis: Continuous Benzathine benzylpenicillin every 3 weeks for at least 5 years or until age 18-21 to prevent recurrences. |
| Diabetes | Type 1 (Immune-mediated), Type 2 (Insulin resistance), Gestational (GDM), and Malnutrition-related diabetes. | Host: Genetics (HLA-DR3/DR4), age, obesity. Environment: Urbanization, sedentary lifestyle. Prevalence 13-15% in India. | Behavioral: Overnutrition (high sucrose/fats), obesity, physical inactivity, alcohol, smoking, low dietary fiber. Biological: Metabolic syndrome. | Genetic predisposition (family history), age, maternal diabetes (IUGR), HLA markers. | Polyuria, polydipsia (thirst), polyphagia (hunger), weight loss, blurred vision, slow-healing sores, ketoacidosis (Type 1), nephropathy. | WHO/IDF: Fasting ≥ 126 mg/dl ( 7.0 mmol/l) or 2-h post-load ≥ 200 mg/dl ( 11.1 mmol/l). GDM: ≥ 140 mg/dl ( 75 g load). HbA1c ≥ 6.5% . | Medical Nutrition Therapy (MNT), oral hypoglycemic drugs (Metformin), insulin, exercise, foot care. | Primordial: Healthy lifestyle in childhood. Primary: Weight control, high-fiber diet ( >30-40 g), 30 mins physical activity daily. | Screening high-risk groups (age >30 ), monitoring HbA1c, GDM screening in pregnancy ( <12 weeks), and early diagnosis to prevent retinopathy. |
| Obesity | Overweight, Abdominal (central/android) obesity, Morbid obesity, Metabolic syndrome. | Host: Genetic traits, age, sex (higher in females), multiparity. Agent: Energy intake > expenditure. Environment: Junk food availability, sedentary urban culture. | Behavioral: Unhealthy eating (high fat/sugar), sedentary habits, lack of regular exercise, high oil/alcohol consumption. Biological: Endocrine factors. | Genetic/Constitutional predisposition ( 50-60% influence), age (metabolic changes). | Excessive body fat accumulation, breathlessness, lethargy, sleep apnea, back pain, increased risk of osteoarthritis. | BMI: Weight(kg) / Height(m)2 . Overweight ≥ 25 , Obese ≥ 30 (Asian cut-offs: 23 and 25 ). Waist circumference: M > 102 cm, W > 88 cm. | Calorie restriction, increased physical activity, behavior modification, bariatric surgery (gastric bypass), appetite-suppressing drugs. | Primordial: Health education in schools for healthy lifestyles. Primary: Balanced diet ( 500-600 g fruits/veg), avoiding junk foods, regular exercise. | Screening for BMI and waist circumference, and weight reduction (target 5-10% ) to prevent complications like IHD and diabetes. |
| Blindness | Visual impairment ( <6/18 ), Economic blindness ( <6/60 ), Social blindness ( <3/60 ), Low vision, Nutritional blindness. | Host: Age ( >50 years), sex (females higher due to cataract/trachoma), illiteracy. Environment: Poor sanitation, rural areas, UV light, dust/smoke. | Nutritional: Vitamin A deficiency. Behavioral: Poor eye hygiene, smoking, occupational hazards (welding, stone breaking). Biological: Uncontrolled diabetes. | Age (cataract/glaucoma), genetic predisposition (retinitis pigmentosa), congenital anomalies. | Night blindness ( XN ), Bitot's spots ( X1B ), corneal scarring/opacity, cloudy vision (cataract), loss of visual field, white pupillary reflex. | Visual acuity using Snellen's chart, ophthalmoscopy, Slit-lamp examination, NPCB survey criteria. | Cataract surgery (IOL implant), refractive error correction (spectacles), antibiotics for trachoma, massive dose Vitamin A therapy, corneal grafting. | Health promotion: Eye health education, face washing, sanitation. Specific protection: Massive dose Vit A ( 2 lakh IU 6-monthly), measles immunization. | Early diagnosis through school screening (SES), eye camps for cataract, and mass chemoprophylaxis for trachoma (SAFE strategy). |
| Oral diseases | Dental Caries, Periodontal (gum) disease, Oral Cancer (Lip/Oral), Leukoplakia, Dental Fluorosis. | Host: All ages; Oral cancer most common in Indian males. Agent: S. mutans , tobacco, alcohol. Environment: High sugar marketing, fluoride levels in water. | Behavioral: Tobacco use (smoking/chewing), heavy alcohol, high free sugar intake, poor oral hygiene. Biological: Diabetes (linked to periodontitis). | Genetic factors (cleft lip/palate), age, educational attainment/socioeconomic status. | White/red patches (leukoplakia), mouth ulcers, bleeding gums, tooth cavities, tooth discoloration, halitosis (bad breath), tooth loss. | Clinical dental examination, biopsy for suspected cancer, visual inspection of cavities, water fluoride testing. | Restorative dentistry (fillings), scaling, root canal treatment, surgery/radiotherapy for cancer, defluoridation of water (Nalgonda technique). | Health education on oral hygiene, tobacco cessation (COTPA Act), use of fluorides ( 0.5-0.8 ppm in water/toothpaste), reducing sugar intake. | Early detection of precancerous lesions, school health checkups, and regular dental check-ups every 6 months. |
| Drowning | Accidental drowning, Near drowning, Fatal drowning, Submersion/Immersion injuries. | Host: Age (highest risk in children <5 ), males ( 2x higher). Environment: Proximity to open water (wells, ponds), floods, natural disasters (tsunamis). | Behavioral: Lapses in adult supervision, lack of swimming skills, alcohol use near water, overcrowding in vessels. Environmental: Lack of barriers. | Age (young children), gender (males), natural calamities. | Asphyxia, respiratory arrest, loss of consciousness after ≈ 2 mins, cyanosis, hypothermia, irreversible brain damage after 4-6 mins. | Clinical assessment of respiratory/neurological status and history of submersion. | Immediate rescue, clearing airway, artificial respiration (CPR), stabilization of body temperature (warm chain), oxygen therapy. | Public education on water safety, four-sided pool fencing, covering wells, teaching swimming/survival skills, flood early warning systems. | Prompt first aid, rescue by trained personnel, and rapid transport (108 ambulance) to trauma care centers. |
| Burns | Thermal (flame/scald), Flash, Chemical, Electrical, Radiation; 1st, 2nd, 3rd degree. | Host: Children (1–9 years), women (kitchen), industrial workers. Environment: Unsafe kitchens (open chulhas), poverty/overcrowding. | Behavioral: Careless handling of fire/LPG, unsafe handling of assault chemicals, smoking, alcohol abuse. Environmental: Lack of fire safety measures. | Age (very young/elderly), gender (females higher in certain SEAR cultures). | Erythema (redness), blisters, sloughing of epidermis, charring, severe pain or anesthesia (deep burns), shock (weak pulse), sepsis. | Visual assessment of depth and extent using Rule of Nines for Total Body Surface Area (TBSA). | First aid (cool running water), fluid resuscitation (Parkland formula: 4ml × kg × %TBSA ), wound debridement, antibiotics, skin grafting. | Fire safety education, safe cookstove design, legislation for fire-safety in buildings, industrial safety gear (protective clothing/goggles). | Immediate first aid (avoid oil/haldi), early medical care in specialized burn units, and disability limitation through physiotherapy. |
| Falls | Accidental falls (heights vs same level), leading to fractures or head injuries. | Host: Elderly ( >65-75 ), young children, industrial workers. Environment: Poor lighting, slippery floors, high-rise buildings without guards. | Behavioral: Alcohol/substance use, hazardous working conditions, improper footwear. Biological: Osteoporosis, visual impairment, muscle weakness. | Age (geriatric frailty), physical handicaps, neurological disorders (Parkinson's). | Pain, swelling, bruising, fractures (especially hip/wrist), head injury, loss of mobility. | Clinical examination, standardized fall risk assessments, and X-rays/CT scans to identify fractures/trauma. | Setting of fractures (casting), surgical intervention (ORIF), pain management, physical rehabilitation. | Regular exercise for balance/strength, home safety modifications (grab bars, non-slip floors), adequate Vitamin D/calcium intake. | Screening for osteoporosis, fall prevention programs (muscle strengthening), and prompt medical care to restore mobility. |
| Poisoning | Accidental, Suicidal, Homicidal; Chemical (pesticides, heavy metals), Food, Kerosene. | Host: Children (accidental), farmers/laborers (occupational), adults (suicidal). Agent: Organophosphorus, Lead, Methyl alcohol. Environment: Agricultural areas. | Behavioral: Improper storage of chemicals (within reach), lack of PPE, lack of awareness of toxic plants. Biological: Mental health status. | Age (children), occupation. | Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, miosis/mydriasis (pupil changes), convulsions, respiratory failure, specific chemical odors. | History of exposure, chemical analysis of gastric lavage/vomitus, blood toxicology (e.g., serum cholinesterase for organophosphates). | Emergency resuscitation, gastric lavage, specific antidotes (Atropine/Pralidoxime), activated charcoal, supportive care (IV fluids). | Safe storage (high shelves, labels), use of biopesticides, legislation against toxins (lead-free petrol), mental health support. | Early identification of signs, prompt first aid (inducing vomiting if indicated), rapid transport to medical facilities with antidotes. |
| Snake bite | Envenoming (Neurotoxic, Vasculotoxic/Hemotoxic) vs Non-envenoming. | Host: Farmers, agricultural laborers, children. Environment: Rural areas, monsoon season (floods), grassy terrains. | Behavioral: Walking barefoot in fields, sleeping on the floor, handling firewood without protection, late reporting. | Occupation, geographical location, species of snake native to area. | Fang marks, local pain/swelling, ptosis (eyelid drooping), paralysis (neurotoxic), bleeding from gums (hemotoxic), respiratory paralysis. | Clinical signs of envenomation, 20-minute whole blood clotting test (20WBCT), identification of the snake if possible. | First aid (reassurance, immobilization/splint), avoid tourniquets/incisions, administration of Polyvalent Anti-Snake Venom (ASV) intravenously. | Wearing protective boots/long pants, using torches at night, clearing vegetation around homes, using mosquito nets/beds. | National snake-bite protocols (CARRY NO R.I.G.H.T.), rapid transport to PHC/hospital for ASV, wound management. |
