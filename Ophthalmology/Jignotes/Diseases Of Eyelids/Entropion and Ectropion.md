# Diseases of the Eyelids: Entropion and Ectropion

## I. Entropion
**Definition:** Entropion refers to the inward rolling or rotation of the eyelid margin towards the globe. This results in the eyelashes rubbing against the cornea and conjunctiva, a condition known as [[Pseudotrichiasis]].

### Classification and Etiology
Entropion is classified into four main types based on etiology:

1.  **Involutional (Senile) Entropion**
    *   **Incidence:** Most common type; affects only the **lower lid** in elderly people.
    *   **Pathogenesis:** Caused by age-related degenerative changes:
        *   **Horizontal Laxity:** Weakening of the [[Orbicularis Oculi]] and canthal tendons. Tested by *Snap-back test* and *Pinch test*.
        *   **Vertical Instability:** Attenuation, dehiscence, or disinsertion of lower lid retractors (capsulopalpebral fascia).
        *   **Preseptal Overriding:** The preseptal orbicularis muscle overrides the pretarsal orbicularis during lid closure due to degeneration of palpebral connective tissue.
        *   **Orbital Septum Laxity:** Prolapse of orbital fat.

2.  **Cicatricial Entropion**
    *   **Incidence:** Affects both upper and lower lids.
    *   **Pathogenesis:** Caused by scarring and contraction of the [[Palpebral Conjunctiva]] and distortion of the tarsal plate.
    *   **Causes:** [[Trachoma]] (commonest), [[Stevens-Johnson Syndrome]], [[Chemical Burns]], [[Pemphigoid]].

3.  **Spastic (Acute) Entropion**
    *   Affects the lower lid. Usually due to ocular irritation leading to [[Blepharospasm]] (sustained contraction of orbicularis). Common in patients with ocular inflammation or after tight bandaging.

4.  **Congenital Entropion**
    *   **Rare.** Usually affects the lower lid due to improper development of inferior retractor aponeurosis. Upper lid involvement is often secondary to microphthalmos.

### Clinical Features
*   **Symptoms:** Foreign body sensation, irritation, lacrimation ([[Epiphora]]), photophobia.
*   **Signs:**
    *   In-rolling of the lid margin.
    *   **Grade I:** Only posterior lid border is in-rolled.
    *   **Grade II:** In-turning up to the inter-marginal strip.
    *   **Grade III:** Whole lid margin including anterior border is in-turned.
    *   **Secondary Changes:** Conjunctival congestion, corneal abrasions, superficial corneal opacities, vascularization, and corneal ulceration.

### Management

**A. Congenital Entropion**
*   May resolve spontaneously.
*   **Hotz procedure:** Excision of skin and muscle strip with reconstruction of lid crease.

**B. Acute Spastic Entropion**
*   Taping of the lid.
*   Lid everting sutures.
*   Botulinum toxin injection.

**C. Involutional (Senile) Entropion**
*   **Temporary:** Transverse lid-everting sutures.
*   **Surgical Procedures:**
    1.  **Fox Procedure:** Excision of a base-down triangle of conjunctiva and tarsal plate.
    2.  **Weis Procedure:** Full-thickness horizontal lid splitting with marginal rotation.
    3.  **Modified Wheeler’s Operation:** Preseptal orbicularis bracing.
    4.  **Jones/Reeh Operation:** Plication (shortening) of lower lid retractors.
    5.  **Quickert Procedure:** Combines horizontal shortening with the Weis procedure (transverse lid split + everting sutures).
    6.  **Lateral Tarsal Strip:** For marked horizontal laxity.

**D. Cicatricial Entropion**
*   **Anterior Lamellar Resection:** For mild cases.
*   **Tarsal Wedge Resection:** For moderate entropion with atrophic tarsus.
*   **Tarsal Fracture (Modified Ketssey’s Operation):** Transposition of tarsoconjunctival wedge. Useful for mild to moderate cases.
*   **Posterior Lamellar Graft:** Replacement of scarred conjunctiva/tarsus with mucous membrane graft, hard palate, or ear cartilage.

## II. Ectropion
**Definition:** Outward turning or eversion of the eyelid margin. This leads to exposure of the palpebral conjunctiva and bulbar conjunctiva.

### Classification and Etiology
1.  **Involutional (Senile) Ectropion**
    *   **Incidence:** Most common type; affects only the **lower lid**.
    *   **Pathogenesis:** Horizontal lid laxity, medial/lateral canthal tendon laxity, and dehiscence of lower lid retractors.

2.  **Cicatricial Ectropion**
    *   Affects both upper and lower lids.
    *   **Causes:** Scarring of the skin due to thermal/chemical burns, trauma, ulcers, or skin infections. Vertical shortage of the anterior lamella (skin) pulls the lid outward.

3.  **Paralytic Ectropion**
    *   Affects the lower lid.
    *   **Cause:** [[Facial Nerve Palsy]] (CN VII) leading to loss of orbicularis oculi tone.

4.  **Mechanical Ectropion**
    *   Caused by the weight of a tumor (e.g., neurofibroma), large chalazion, or marked edema pushing the lid outward.

5.  **Congenital Ectropion**
    *   Rare. Usually associated with [[Blepharophimosis Syndrome]] or Down syndrome due to shortage of skin.

### Clinical Features
*   **Symptoms:**
    *   **Epiphora:** Main symptom (due to eversion of the punctum).
    *   Chronic conjunctivitis symptoms (irritation, discharge).
*   **Signs:**
    *   **Grade I:** Only punctum is everted.
    *   **Grade II:** Lid margin everted, palpebral conjunctiva visible.
    *   **Grade III:** Fornix is visible.
    *   **Complications:** Eczema of lower lid skin, conjunctival xerosis/keratinization, [[Exposure Keratitis]].

### Management

**A. Involutional Ectropion**
*   **Mild (Medial Ectropion):**
    *   **Ziegler Cautery Punctures:** To induce scarring and inversion.
    *   **Medial Conjunctivoplasty:** Excision of spindle-shaped conjunctiva below the punctum.
*   **Moderate:**
    *   **Horizontal Lid Shortening:** Full-thickness pentagonal excision (e.g., Bick procedure).
*   **Severe/Extensive:**
    *   **Modified Kuhnt-Szymanowski Operation:** Combines horizontal shortening with blepharoplasty (skin excision).
    *   **Lateral Tarsal Strip Procedure:** For generalized ectropion with horizontal laxity.

**B. Cicatricial Ectropion**
*   **Mild:** V-Y Operation (Wharton Jones) or Z-plasty (Elschnig’s operation) to relax the scar line.
*   **Severe:** Excision of scar tissue followed by full-thickness **skin grafting** (from upper lid or retroauricular area).

**C. Paralytic Ectropion**
*   **Temporary:** Lubricants, taping the lid, suture tarsorrhaphy.
*   **Permanent:**
    *   Medial/Lateral Canthoplasty.
    *   **Fascia Lata Sling Operation:** To support the lower lid.

## III. Summary Comparison

| Feature | Entropion | Ectropion |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Definition** | Inward turning of lid margin | Outward turning of lid margin |
| **Primary Symptom** | Foreign body sensation (Trichiasis) | Epiphora (Watering) |
| **Commonest Type** | Involutional (Senile) | Involutional (Senile) |
| **Lid Affected (Senile)**| Lower lid | Lower lid |
| **Key Mechanism** | Overriding of preseptal orbicularis | Horizontal lid laxity & gravity |
| **Corneal Risk** | Ulceration (mechanical rubbing) | Exposure Keratitis (drying) |
| **Specific Surgery** | Weis procedure, Jones plication | Kuhnt-Szymanowski, V-Y Plasty |

**Clinical Implication:** Both conditions can lead to vision-threatening corneal complications. Entropion carries a higher immediate risk of corneal abrasion and infectious keratitis due to lashes rubbing the cornea. Ectropion primarily causes exposure keratopathy and troublesome epiphora.
