# Glaucoma Types, Etiopathogenesis, and Treatment

[[Glaucoma]] is a group of ocular disorders characterized by progressive [[optic neuropathy]] with characteristic [[optic disc]] changes and [[visual field defects]], usually (but not always) associated with raised [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP). It is broadly classified based on the anatomy of the [[anterior chamber angle]] (Open vs. Closed) and the underlying cause (Primary vs. Secondary).

### I. Primary Glaucomas

These occur without any obvious systemic or ocular cause for the rise in IOP.

| Type / Subtype | Defect / Clinical Features | Etiopathogenesis | Treatment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[[Primary Open Angle Glaucoma]] (POAG)** | • **Defect:** Chronic, progressive [[optic neuropathy]] with an **open angle** on [[gonioscopy]].<br>• **Signs:** IOP > 21 mmHg, [[optic disc cupping]] (vertical CDR > 0.5), [[bayonetting sign]], [[laminar dot sign]], and visual field defects (e.g., [[Bjerrum scotoma]], [[nasal step]]). | • **Mechanism:** Increased resistance to aqueous outflow at the **trabecular meshwork** (specifically juxtacanalicular tissue) and collapse of [[Schlemm's canal]].<br>• **Risk Factors:** Age >40, genetics (Myocilin, Optineurin genes), myopia, diabetes. | • **Medical (1st Line):** [[Prostaglandin analogues]] (e.g., Latanoprost) or [[Beta-blockers]] (Timolol).<br>• **Laser:** [[Argon Laser Trabeculoplasty]] (ALT) or Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT).<br>• **Surgical:** [[Trabeculectomy]] (Gold standard filtration surgery). |
| **[[Normal Tension Glaucoma]] (NTG)** | • **Defect:** Glaucomatous disc damage and field defects with **IOP consistently ≤ 21 mmHg**.<br>• **Signs:** Splinter hemorrhages on the disc are more common than in POAG. | • **Mechanism:** Vascular insufficiency to the [[optic nerve head]] or structural weakness of the [[lamina cribrosa]].<br>• **Associations:** Migraine, Raynaud’s phenomenon, nocturnal hypotension, sleep apnea. | • **Goal:** Lower IOP by 30% from baseline.<br>• **Medical:** PG analogues, Brimonidine (neuroprotective potential). Calcium channel blockers may help vascular perfusion.<br>• **Surgical:** Trabeculectomy if progressive. |
| **[[Primary Angle Closure Glaucoma]] (PACG)** | • **Defect:** Apposition of the peripheral [[iris]] against the [[trabecular meshwork]], obstructing outflow.<br>• **Stages:** Suspect (PACS) $\rightarrow$ Closure (PAC) $\rightarrow$ Glaucoma (PACG).<br>• **Acute Attack:** Pain, redness, halos, hazy cornea, mid-dilated fixed pupil, [[Vogt's triad]]. | • **Mechanism:** **Relative Pupillary Block** (most common). Aqueous accumulates in posterior chamber $\rightarrow$ iris bombs forward $\rightarrow$ angle closes.<br>• **Risk Factors:** Hypermetropia, short axial length, shallow anterior chamber, plateau iris. | • **Acute:** IV [[Mannitol]], Acetazolamide, [[Pilocarpine]] (once IOP < 40 mmHg), Topical steroids.<br>• **Definitive:** **Laser Peripheral Iridotomy (LPI)** (Nd:YAG).<br>• **Surgical:** Trabeculectomy or Lens extraction if cataractous. |
| **[[Plateau Iris Syndrome]]** | • **Defect:** Angle closure despite a patent iridotomy.<br>• **Signs:** Deep central AC but narrow peripheral angle; "Double hump" sign on indentation gonioscopy. | • **Mechanism:** Anteriorly positioned [[ciliary processes]] push the peripheral iris forward, closing the angle (Non-pupillary block mechanism). | • **Laser:** Argon Laser Peripheral Iridoplasty (ALPI).<br>• **Medical:** Miotics (Pilocarpine) long-term. |

**Clinical Implication:** In [[Acute Congestive Glaucoma]], **Pilocarpine** is ineffective if IOP > 40 mmHg because the iris sphincter is ischemic. Lower IOP with secretory inhibitors or hyperosmotics first.

### II. Secondary Glaucomas

Glaucoma resulting from a specific ocular or systemic disease.

#### A. Lens-Induced Glaucomas

| Subtype | Defect / Clinical Features | Etiopathogenesis | Treatment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[[Phacomorphic Glaucoma]]** | • **Type:** Secondary Angle Closure.<br>• **Signs:** Shallow AC, swollen lens, pain. | • **Mechanism:** Intumescent (swollen) cataract pushes the iris forward, blocking the angle. | • **Medical:** Lower IOP.<br>• **Definitive:** Cataract extraction (surgery). |
| **[[Phacolytic Glaucoma]]** | • **Type:** Secondary Open Angle.<br>• **Signs:** Deep AC, floating white particles (lens protein + macrophages), Morgagnian cataract. | • **Mechanism:** Leakage of lens proteins from a hypermature cataract through an intact capsule. Macrophages engulf proteins and clog the [[trabecular meshwork]]. | • **Definitive:** Cataract extraction with AC wash. |
| **[[Lens Particle Glaucoma]]** | • **Type:** Secondary Open Angle.<br>• **Signs:** Cortical matter in AC following trauma or surgery. | • **Mechanism:** Broken lens capsule releases lens cortex particles which block the trabecular meshwork. | • Irrigation and aspiration of lens matter. |
| **[[Phacoanaphylactic Glaucoma]]** | • **Type:** Granulomatous Uveitis leading to glaucoma.<br>• **Signs:** KP's, hypopyon after capsule rupture. | • **Mechanism:** Autoimmune reaction (Type IV hypersensitivity) to lens protein causing inflammation. | • Steroids and removal of lens remnants. |
| **[[Phacotopic Glaucoma]]** | • **Type:** Secondary Angle Closure.<br>• **Signs:** Subluxated/dislocated lens (e.g., Microspherophakia). | • **Mechanism:** Dislocated lens blocks the pupil or pushes iris forward. | • Peripheral Iridotomy (PI) or Lensectomy. |

#### B. Other Important Secondary Glaucomas

| Type | Defect / Features | Etiopathogenesis | Treatment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[[Pseudoexfoliation Glaucoma]]** | • **Defect:** Most common secondary Open Angle Glaucoma.<br>• **Signs:** **Target sign** on lens, Sampaolesi line (pigmented Schwalbe's line), "Moth-eaten" sphincter. | • **Gene:** **LOX-L1** mutation.<br>• **Mech:** Dandruff-like exfoliative material deposits in the TM, clogging it. | • Similar to POAG (Meds/Sx).<br>• Often requires aggressive management. |
| **[[Pigmentary Glaucoma]]** | • **Defect:** Secondary Open Angle.<br>• **Signs:** **Krukenberg spindle** (corneal endothelium), mid-peripheral iris transillumination defects, Sampaolesi line. | • **Mech:** Mechanical rubbing of posterior iris pigment against zonules (in young myopic males) $\rightarrow$ pigment dispersion $\rightarrow$ TM blockage. | • Miotics (Pilocarpine reduces iris-zonule contact).<br>• Laser Trabeculoplasty.<br>• Filtration surgery. |
| **[[Neovascular Glaucoma]] (NVG)** | • **Defect:** Secondary Angle Closure (Zipper closure).<br>• **Signs:** **Rubeosis Iridis** (NVI), high IOP, hyphema. | • **Mech:** Retinal ischemia (CRVO, PDR) $\rightarrow$ VEGF release $\rightarrow$ new vessels on iris/angle $\rightarrow$ fibrovascular membrane contracts and closes angle. | • **Prophylaxis:** Pan-Retinal Photocoagulation (PRP).<br>• **Rx:** Anti-VEGF, Glaucoma Drainage Devices (Ahmed Valve). Cyclodestruction in blind painful eye. |
| **[[Inflammatory Glaucoma]] (Uveitic)** | • **Defect:** Secondary Open or Closed.<br>• **Signs:** KP's, cells/flare, posterior synechiae. | • **Open:** TM clogging by cells/debris or trabeculitis.<br>• **Closed:** Seclusio pupillae (iris bombe) or PAS formation. | • **Rx Uveitis:** Steroids + Cycloplegics.<br>• **Rx IOP:** Aqueous suppressants (Beta-blockers, CAIs). **Avoid Pilocarpine/PG analogs**. |
| **[[Posner-Schlossman Syndrome]]** | • **Defect:** Glaucomatocyclitic Crisis.<br>• **Signs:** Unilateral, recurrent mild uveitis, **very high IOP**, open angle, few KP's. | • **Mech:** Trabeculitis (inflammation of TM). Possible viral etiology (CMV/HSV). | • Steroids (topical) + Aqueous suppressants.<br>• Self-limiting but recurrent. |
| **[[Malignant Glaucoma]]** | • **Defect:** **Ciliary Block Glaucoma** (Inverse Glaucoma).<br>• **Signs:** **Flat Anterior Chamber** despite patent PI, high IOP post-surgery. | • **Mech:** Misdirection of aqueous posteriorly into vitreous $\rightarrow$ anterior rotation of ciliary body $\rightarrow$ iris-lens diaphragm pushed forward. | • **Medical:** **Atropine** (Cycloplegia relaxes ciliary muscle, pulls lens back), IV Mannitol.<br>• **Laser:** YAG Hyaloidotomy.<br>• **Surgical:** Vitrectomy. |
| **[[Steroid Induced Glaucoma]]** | • **Defect:** Secondary Open Angle.<br>• **Signs:** Looks like POAG. History of steroid use (topical > systemic). | • **Mech:** Accumulation of glycosaminoglycans (GAGs) in TM $\rightarrow$ increased outflow resistance. **Responders:** 5% population are high responders. | • Stop steroids (if possible).<br>• Anti-glaucoma meds.<br>• Switch to "soft" steroids (Fluorometholone, Loteprednol). |
| **[[Ghost Cell Glaucoma]]** | • **Defect:** Secondary Open Angle.<br>• **Signs:** Tan/Khaki colored cells in AC. | • **Mech:** Degenerated RBCs (Ghost cells) from old vitreous hemorrhage enter AC and block TM. | • AC Washout / Vitrectomy. |

**Clinical Implication:** [[Malignant Glaucoma]] is called "Inverse Glaucoma" because unlike PACG (where Pilocarpine is used), **Atropine** is the treatment of choice to relax the ciliary muscle and deepen the anterior chamber.

### III. Congenital / Developmental Glaucomas

| Type | Defect / Clinical Features | Etiopathogenesis | Treatment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[[Primary Congenital Glaucoma]]** | • **Signs:** **Buphthalmos** (Ox eye), **Haab’s Striae** (horizontal breaks in Descemet’s), Photophobia, Lacrimation, Blepharospasm.<br>• **Onset:** < 3 years age. | • **Mech:** **Trabeculodysgenesis**. Barkan’s membrane covers the angle, or improper insertion of iris into TM. | • **Surgical (TOC):**<br>1. **Goniotomy** (if cornea clear).<br>2. **Trabeculotomy** (if cornea hazy).<br>3. Combined Trabeculotomy + Trabeculectomy. |
| **[[Axenfeld-Rieger Syndrome]]** | • **Signs:** Posterior embryotoxon (prominent Schwalbe’s line), iris strands, corectopia, polycoria, dental/facial anomalies. | • **Mech:** Iridocorneal dysgenesis (Neural crest cell defect). | • Medical or Surgical (often difficult to manage). |
| **[[Aniridia]]** | • **Signs:** Bilateral absence of iris, foveal hypoplasia (nystagmus), photophobia. | • **Mech:** Stump of iris blocks the TM (50% develop glaucoma). | • Medical or Glaucoma Drainage Devices (GDD). |

**Mnemonic for Congenital Glaucoma Symptoms:**
**B-L-P**
*   **B**lepharospasm
*   **L**acrimation
*   **P**hotophobia
