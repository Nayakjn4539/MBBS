# [[Pediatric Orthopaedics]] > Osteogenesis Imperfecta

# Osteogenesis Imperfecta (Brittle Bone Disease)

### Synonyms
*   Fragilitas Ossium
*   Periosteal Dysplasia
*   Vrolik’s Disease (Congenita/Lethal form)
*   Lobstein’s Disease (Tarda/Mild form)

### Definition
[[Osteogenesis Imperfecta]] (OI) is a hereditary generalized connective tissue disorder characterized by fragile bones that fracture easily, often with trivial or no trauma. It is primarily a defect in the maturation and organization of [[Type I Collagen]].

---

### Etiopathogenesis
*   **Genetic Basis:**
    *   Most commonly **Autosomal Dominant** inheritance.
    *   Mutation in the **COL1A1** or **COL1A2** genes.
    *   Some severe forms may be Autosomal Recessive or result from spontaneous mutation.
*   **Pathology:**
    *   **Defect:** Quantitative or qualitative defect in **Type I Collagen** (the principal protein of bone organic matrix).
    *   **Histology:**
        *   Periosteum is thick, but the cambium layer (osteogenic layer) is thin.
        *   **Cortices:** Thin (pencil-line cortex).
        *   **Trabeculae:** Thin and sparse.
        *   **Marrow:** Fatty and fibrous.
        *   **Healing:** Fractures heal rapidly with abundant (sometimes florid) callus, but the new bone formed is of poor quality (woven bone).

**Clinical Implication:** Because Type I collagen is ubiquitous, extraskeletal tissues like the sclera, teeth, ligaments, and skin are also affected.

---

### Classification

#### 1. Clinical Classification (Traditional)
| Type | Characteristics | Prognosis |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **OI Congenita** (Vrolik’s) | Fractures occur *in utero*. Child born with deformities. | High mortality (often stillborn or early neonatal death). |
| **OI Tarda** (Lobstein’s) | Fractures occur after birth (often when child starts walking). | Better survival; fractures decrease after puberty. |

#### 2. Sillence Classification (Most Accepted)
| Type | Severity | Inheritance | Clinical Features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Type I** | Mild | Autosomal Dominant | Most common. **Blue sclerae**. Fragile bones. Preserved stature. |
| **Type II** | Lethal | Autosomal Recessive | **Perinatal lethal**. Multiple intrauterine fractures. "Crumpled" long bones. |
| **Type III** | Severe | Autosomal Recessive/Dominant | **Progressive deforming**. Normal sclerae (or grey). Severe dwarfism. Triangular face. |
| **Type IV** | Moderate | Autosomal Dominant | Variable severity. **Normal sclerae**. Moderate deformity. |

---

### Clinical Features

#### A. Skeletal Manifestations
1.  **Fragile Bones:** Multiple fractures with minimal trauma.
2.  **Deformities:**
    *   Bowing of long bones (e.g., Sabre shin appearance of tibia).
    *   Malunion of fractures.
    *   Kyphoscoliosis of the spine.
3.  **Short Stature:** Disproportionate dwarfism (in severe types).
4.  **Skull:**
    *   Broad skull with triangular face.
    *   Frontal bossing (overhanging occiput).
    *   Delayed closure of fontanelles.

#### B. Extraskeletal Manifestations
1.  **Eye:** [[Blue Sclera]] (The thin sclera allows the underlying choroidal pigment to show through).
2.  **Ear:** Conductive deafness (Otosclerosis) in 2nd–3rd decade.
3.  **Teeth:** [[Dentinogenesis Imperfecta]] (Teeth are brownish/translucent, brittle, and prone to caries).
4.  **Joints:** Ligamentous laxity and hypermobility.
5.  **Skin:** Thin, translucent skin; easy bruising.
6.  **Cardiovascular:** Mitral valve prolapse (rare).

**Mnemonic: BLOOD**
*   **B** - Blue sclerae
*   **L** - Laxity of joints
*   **O** - Otosclerosis (Deafness)
*   **O** - Osteoporosis (Fragile bones)
*   **D** - Dentinogenesis Imperfecta

**Mnemonic: 3 F's**
*   **F**ragile bones
*   **F**ractures (Multiple)
*   **F**etal variety is **F**atal

### Investigations

#### 1. Radiology (X-rays)
*   **General:** Generalized [[Osteoporosis]] (diffuse rarefaction).
*   **Long Bones:**
    *   **Pencil-line cortices:** Extremely thin cortex.
    *   **Deformities:** Bowing and angulation.
    *   **Fractures:** Multiple fractures in various stages of healing with callus.
    *   **Popcorn calcification:** Seen at the metaphysis/epiphysis.
    *   **Cystic changes:** Honeycomb appearance in severe cases.
*   **Skull:**
    *   **Wormian bones:** Multiple small irregular bones in the sutures (Mosaic pattern).
    *   Tam-o'-shanter skull shape.
*   **Spine:**
    *   Biconcave vertebrae (**Codfish vertebrae**) due to disc pressure on soft bone.
    *   Platyspondyly (flattened vertebrae).

#### 2. Laboratory Tests
*   **Serum Calcium & Phosphorus:** Usually **Normal**.
*   **Serum Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP):** May be elevated (due to multiple healing fractures), otherwise normal.
*   **Urinary Hydroxyproline:** Increased (indicates high collagen turnover).

#### 3. Prenatal Diagnosis
*   **Ultrasonography:** Can detect limb shortening and multiple fractures in Type II (Lethal) OI in the second trimester.

---

### Differential Diagnosis
1.  **[[Non-Accidental Injury]] (Battered Baby Syndrome):**
    *   *Crucial distinction:* In child abuse, look for inconsistent history, bruises in different stages of healing, and posterior rib fractures. OI presents with osteopenia and specific features like blue sclera.
2.  **[[Rickets]]:** Features like cupping/splaying of metaphysis and biochemical changes (low Ca/PO4, High ALP) distinguish it.
3.  **Congenital Hypophosphatasia.**

---

### Management

There is no cure. Treatment is supportive, aiming to reduce fracture frequency, prevent deformity, and maximize function.

#### 1. Medical Management
*   **[[Bisphosphonates]] (e.g., Pamidronate, Zoledronic acid):**
    *   **Mechanism:** Inhibit osteoclastic bone resorption.
    *   **Outcome:** Increase bone mineral density (BMD), reduce pain, and decrease fracture incidence.
    *   **Administration:** Cyclic intravenous infusion or oral.
*   **Supplements:** Calcium and Vitamin D.
*   **Newer Therapies:** Denosumab (RANKL inhibitor), Teriparatide (in adults).

#### 2. Orthopaedic Management
*   **Fracture Care:**
    *   Conservative management with lightweight splints or casts.
    *   Avoid prolonged immobilization to prevent disuse osteoporosis.
*   **Correction of Deformities:**
    *   **[[Sofield-Millar Operation]] (Seekh-Kebab Procedure):**
        *   Multiple osteotomies are performed to straighten the bone.
        *   Fragments are threaded over an intramedullary rod.
    *   **Intramedullary Nailing:**
        *   **Telescopic Nails (e.g., [[Bailey-Dubow Rod]], Fassier-Duval Rod):** These expand as the child grows, preventing the need for repeated surgeries.
        *   **Fixed Nails (e.g., Rush nail, K-nail):** Used but require replacement as the bone outgrows the nail.

#### 3. Rehabilitation
*   Bracing (Orthotics) for support.
*   Physiotherapy to strengthen muscles and improve gait.
*   Gentle handling advice for parents to prevent iatrogenic fractures.
