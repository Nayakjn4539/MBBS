# [[Sinus]] > Acute Sinusitis

# **Acute Rhinosinusitis (ARS)**

### **Definition**
**[[Acute Rhinosinusitis]]** is defined as the inflammation of the mucous membrane of the nose and one or more [[paranasal sinuses]] characterized by symptoms lasting less than **12 weeks** (some sources say 4 weeks) with complete resolution of symptoms.,
*   The term "Rhinosinusitis" is preferred over "Sinusitis" because the nasal mucosa and sinus mucosa are continuous, and inflammation rarely affects the sinuses without affecting the nose.,

### **Classification**
Based on duration and etiology:
1.  **Acute Viral Rhinosinusitis ([[Common cold]]):** Symptoms lasting < 10 days.,
2.  **Acute Post-viral Rhinosinusitis:** Increase in symptoms after 5 days or persistent symptoms after 10 days.
3.  **[[Acute Bacterial Rhinosinusitis]] (ABRS):** Symptoms persist or worsen beyond 10 days ("Double sickening" pattern).,,

### **Etiology**
#### **1. Viral (Most Common)**
*   **[[Rhinovirus]]** (>50%)
*   **[[Coronavirus]]**
*   **[[Influenza virus]]**
*   **[[Parainfluenza virus]]**
*   **[[Respiratory syncytial virus]] (RSV)**,

#### **2. Bacterial (ABRS)**
Usually follows a viral infection. Common pathogens include:
*   **[[Streptococcus pneumoniae]]** (Most common, ~30–60%),,
*   **[[Haemophilus influenzae]]** (20%)
*   **[[Moraxella catarrhalis]]**,
*   *[[Staphylococcus aureus]]*
*   *[[Streptococcus pyogenes]]*
*   **Anaerobes**: Often seen in sinusitis of dental origin.,

#### **3. Fungal**
*   Rare in acute settings, except in immunocompromised patients (e.g., **[[Invasive Fungal Sinusitis]]**).,

#### **Predisposing Factors**
*   **Local**: **[[Deviated nasal septum]]**, **[[Nasal polyps]]**, **[[Concha bullosa]]**, foreign bodies, trauma.,
*   **Systemic**: Immune deficiency, **[[Ciliary dyskinesia]]** (e.g., **[[Kartagener's syndrome]]**), **[[Cystic fibrosis]]**.,
*   **Environmental**: Smoking, pollution, swimming/diving (infected water enters ostia).,
*   **Dental**: Apical abscesses or extraction of upper molars/premolars (leads to **[[Maxillary sinusitis]]**).,

---

### **Pathophysiology**
The key mechanism involves obstruction of the **[[Osteomeatal complex]]**.
1.  **Ostial Obstruction**: Mucosal edema (viral/allergic) blocks the natural drainage of the sinus.
2.  **Stasis of Secretions**: Negative pressure develops, leading to transudation of fluid and stasis.,
3.  **Hypoxia and Ciliary Dysfunction**: Reduced oxygen tension impairs ciliary movement.
4.  **Bacterial Proliferation**: Stagnant secretions act as a culture medium for secondary bacterial infection.,

### **Clinical Features**
Diagnosis requires the presence of **two or more** symptoms, one of which must be nasal blockage or discharge.

#### **Major Symptoms**
*   **[[Nasal obstruction]]** / congestion / blockage.
*   **[[Nasal discharge]]** (Anterior or **[[Postnasal drip]]**): Often mucopurulent or discolored.,
*   **[[Facial pain]]** or pressure.
*   Reduction or loss of **[[Smell]]** (**[[Hyposmia]]** / **[[Anosmia]]**).

#### **Specific Sinus Presentations**
| Sinus Involved | Location of Pain/Headache | Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[[Maxillary Sinusitis]]** (Most Common in Adults) | Cheek (Maxilla), Upper teeth | Pain referred to upper teeth; worse on bending forward or straining.,, |
| **[[Frontal Sinusitis]]** | Forehead (Supraorbital) | **"Office Headache"** (Periodicity): Starts in morning, peaks at noon, subsides by evening., Tenderness floor of frontal sinus (Ewing's sign). |
| **[[Ethmoid Sinusitis]]** (Common in Children) | Bridge of nose, medial to eye | Pain deep to the eye; aggravated by eye movements. Associated with lid edema.,, |
| **[[Sphenoid Sinusitis]]** | Occipital, Vertex, Retro-orbital | Deep-seated headache; often described as "vertex" or "occipital" pain.,, |

**Clinical Implication:** In children, cough (day and night) is a significant symptom instead of smell disturbance.,

---

### **Diagnosis**
*   **Clinical**: Diagnosis is primarily based on history and examination.
*   **"Double Sickening" Phenomenon**: Initial improvement of a viral URI followed by worsening of symptoms suggests **[[Acute Bacterial Rhinosinusitis]]**.,
*   **[[Anterior Rhinoscopy]]**: May show congested mucosa, pus in the middle meatus, or hypertrophied turbinates.,
*   **[[Diagnostic Nasal Endoscopy]] (DNE)**: Confirms purulent discharge in the **[[Middle meatus]]** (for anterior sinuses) or **[[Sphenoethmoidal recess]]** (for posterior sinuses).,

#### **Investigations**
*   **[[CT Scan]] (NCCT Nose & PNS)**:
    *   **Gold Standard** for imaging but **NOT** indicated for uncomplicated acute cases.
    *   **Indications**: Suspected complications (orbital/intracranial), severe illness, or failure of medical therapy.,
    *   **Findings**: Air-fluid levels, complete opacification, mucosal thickening.,
*   **X-Ray PNS (Waters' View)**: Shows air-fluid levels or haziness. Largely replaced by CT/Endoscopy due to poor sensitivity.,
*   **Culture**: Only if empirical therapy fails; pus collected via endoscopy or antral tap.

---

### **Treatment**

#### **1. Medical Management**
*   **Symptomatic (Viral)**: Self-limiting (7-10 days). Analgesics, saline irrigation, hydration.
*   **[[Antibiotics]]**:
    *   Indicated for **[[Acute Bacterial Rhinosinusitis]]** (severe symptoms, duration >10 days, or "double sickening").
    *   **First line**: **[[Amoxicillin]]** or **[[Co-amoxiclav]]** (Amoxicillin + Clavulanate).,
    *   **Alternatives**: Cephalosporins (Cefuroxime), Macrolides (in penicillin allergy).,
    *   Duration: usually 10–14 days.
*   **[[Decongestants]]**: Topical (Oxymetazoline/Xylometazoline) for < 5-7 days to prevent **[[Rhinitis medicamentosa]]**; Systemic (Pseudoephedrine).,,
*   **[[Nasal Saline Irrigation]]**: Improves mucociliary clearance.,
*   **[[Intranasal Corticosteroids]]**: Adjunct therapy to reduce mucosal edema.,

#### **2. Surgical Management**
Indicated for impending complications, severe pain not responding to medication, or failed medical therapy.
*   **[[Functional Endoscopic Sinus Surgery]] (FESS)**: Standard for restoring drainage and ventilation.,
*   **[[Antral Lavage]] (Proof Puncture)**: Rarely done now. Used to wash out pus from maxillary sinus.,
*   **[[Frontal Sinus Trephination]]**: For acute frontal sinusitis with severe pain/orbital complications unresponsive to medication. An external opening is made in the floor of the frontal sinus to drain pus.,

---

### **Complications**
Complications arise due to spread of infection via bony erosion, venous thrombophlebitis (valveless veins), or preformed pathways.

#### **A. Orbital Complications (Chandler's Classification)**,,
Most common complication, usually from **[[Ethmoid sinusitis]]** (spreads through thin **[[Lamina papyracea]]**).
1.  **Stage I: [[Preseptal Cellulitis]]**: Eyelid edema, normal eye movement, normal vision.
2.  **Stage II: [[Orbital Cellulitis]]**: Chemosis, proptosis, restricted eye movement, fever.
3.  **Stage III: [[Subperiosteal Abscess]]**: Pus between periorbita and bone; globe displaced (down and out in frontal/ethmoid origin).
4.  **Stage IV: [[Orbital Abscess]]**: Pus within orbital fat; severe proptosis, ophthalmoplegia, risk of blindness.
5.  **Stage V: [[Cavernous Sinus Thrombosis]]**: Bilateral symptoms, cranial nerve palsies (III, IV, VI), high fever.

#### **B. Intracranial Complications**,
*   **[[Meningitis]]** (Most common intracranial complication).
*   **[[Epidural Abscess]]** (Extradural).
*   **[[Subdural Abscess]]**.
*   **[[Brain Abscess]]** (Frontal lobe is common site).
*   **[[Sigmoid Sinus Thrombosis]]**.

#### **C. Bony Complications**
*   **[[Osteomyelitis]]**:
    *   **[[Pott's Puffy Tumor]]**: Osteomyelitis of the frontal bone leading to a subperiosteal abscess on the forehead (doughy swelling).,,
    *   Osteomyelitis of Maxilla: Rare, seen in infants/children.
