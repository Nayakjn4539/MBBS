# [[Radiology And Imaging]] > X-ray Views for Specific Fractures

### General Principles of X-Ray Imaging in Trauma

Before ordering specific views, every orthopedic examination must adhere to the standard diagnostic protocol to avoid missing injuries.

**The "Rule of Twos"**
*   **Two Views:** At least two views at right angles to each other (usually Anteroposterior and Lateral) are essential. A fracture line invisible in one view may be clear in the other.
*   **Two Joints:** The x-ray must include the joint *above* and the joint *below* the fracture site to detect associated subluxations or dislocations (e.g., [[Monteggia fracture-dislocation]]).
*   **Two Limbs:** In children, comparative x-rays of the uninjured limb may be necessary to distinguish fracture lines from normal [[epiphysis]] or growth plates.
*   **Two Occasions:** Some fractures (e.g., [[Scaphoid fracture]], stress fractures) may not be visible immediately. Repeat x-rays after 10–14 days (when bone resorption occurs) may reveal the fracture line.

### Upper Limb Fractures

#### 1. Shoulder and Clavicle
The shoulder is a complex region where overlapping bony shadows can obscure fractures or dislocations.

| Region | Standard Views | Special Views/Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[[Clavicle]]** | • Anteroposterior (AP) <br>• 45° Cephalic tilt | The cephalic tilt view prevents the rib cage from obscuring the clavicle. |
| **[[Shoulder Joint]]** (Proximal Humerus) | • AP (Internal & External rotation)<br>• Lateral (Axillary view or Transthoracic) | **[[Axillary view]]:** Crucial for determining glenohumeral dislocations and fractures of the glenoid rim. <br> **[[Scapular Y view]]:** Useful if the patient cannot abduct the arm for an axillary view. |
| **[[Scapula]]** | • True Scapular AP<br>• True Lateral (Axillary) | Helps to see fractures of the body and neck of the scapula. |

**Clinical Implication:** In [[Posterior shoulder dislocation]], the AP view might look deceptively normal or show the **"Light Bulb Sign"** (humeral head fixed in internal rotation). An axillary or lateral view is mandatory to confirm the head is behind the glenoid.

#### 2. Humerus and Elbow
| Region | Standard Views | Special Views/Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[[Humerus Shaft]]** | • AP<br>• Lateral | Must include shoulder and elbow joints. |
| **[[Elbow Joint]]** | • AP (Elbow extended)<br>• Lateral (Elbow flexed 90°) | **Fat Pad Sign:** In occult fractures (e.g., radial head or supracondylar), displacement of the fat pads (posterior fat pad sign) suggests joint effusion. |
| **[[Supracondylar Fracture]]** | • AP<br>• Lateral | **[[Baumann’s angle]]**: Measured on the AP view to assess varus/valgus alignment. <br> **Anterior Humeral Line**: On lateral view, should pass through the middle third of the capitellum. |
| **[[Radial Head]]** | • AP<br>• Lateral | Radiocapitellar line should point to the capitellum in all views. |

#### 3. Forearm, Wrist, and Hand
| Region | Standard Views | Special Views/Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[[Forearm]]** | • AP<br>• Lateral | Must include wrist and elbow to rule out Galeazzi or Monteggia fracture-dislocations. |
| **[[Distal Radius]]** | • AP<br>• Lateral | Evaluate radial height, radial inclination, and volar tilt. |
| **[[Scaphoid]]** | • AP<br>• Lateral<br>• Oblique<br>• Ziter view (PA with Ulnar deviation) | **Ulnar Deviation View:** Elongates the scaphoid and brings it out of the shadow of other carpal bones. If negative but clinical suspicion is high, repeat X-ray in 2 weeks or MRI. |
| **[[Hand/Fingers]]** | • AP<br>• Lateral<br>• Oblique | **Brewerton View:** For metacarpal head fractures. <br> **Robert’s View:** True AP of the thumb (hyper-pronated) for Bennett’s fracture. |

> [!warning] Diagram Alert
> Scaphoid Views
*Imagine a hand placed flat (AP), then turned sideways (Lateral), then tilted (Oblique), and finally the hand flat but deviated outward towards the ulna (Ziter view). This sequence is critical for capturing the scaphoid waist.*

### Spine and Pelvis

#### 1. Spine
Imaging the spine requires clearing all relevant levels. In trauma, C7-T1 junction visualization is critical.

| Region | Standard Views | Special Views/Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[[Cervical Spine]]** | • AP<br>• Lateral (Must see C1 to T1)<br>• Open Mouth (Odontoid) | **[[Open Mouth View]]**: Specifically visualizes C1 (Atlas) and C2 (Axis) to detect Jefferson fractures or Odontoid fractures. <br> **Swimmer’s View**: To visualize C7-T1 junction if obscured by shoulders. |
| **[[Thoracolumbar Spine]]** | • AP<br>• Lateral | **Oblique Views**: "Scottie Dog" sign for spondylolysis (pars interarticularis defect). |

**Clinical Implication:** In cervical spine trauma, ensure four parallel lines are intact on the lateral view: Anterior vertebral line, Posterior vertebral line, Spinolaminar line, and Spinous process tips.

#### 2. Pelvis and Acetabulum
Standard AP pelvis is the screening tool, but complex fractures require specific angulations.

*   **[[Pelvic Inlet View]]** (Tube angled 40-60° Caudal): Visualizes the pelvic ring integrity, posterior displacement, and inward/outward rotation of the hemipelvis.
*   **[[Pelvic Outlet View]]** (Tube angled 20-45° Cephalad): Visualizes vertical shear fractures, sacral fractures, and superior/inferior displacement.
*   **[[Judet Views]]** (45° Oblique views):
    *   **Iliac Oblique:** Shows the posterior column and anterior wall of the acetabulum.
    *   **Obturator Oblique:** Shows the anterior column and posterior wall of the acetabulum.

### Lower Limb Fractures

#### 1. Hip and Femur
| Region | Standard Views | Special Views/Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[[Hip Joint]]** | • AP Pelvis (showing both hips)<br>• Lateral (Cross-table) | **[[Shenton’s Line]]**: A smooth arc along the inferior femoral neck and superior pubic ramus; break in this line indicates dislocation or fracture. <br> **Frog-leg Lateral**: For slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE) or Perthes, but **avoid** in acute trauma/suspected fracture to prevent displacement. |
| **[[Femur Shaft]]** | • AP<br>• Lateral | Must include hip and knee joints to rule out ipsilateral neck fractures or knee injuries. |

#### 2. Knee
| Region | Standard Views | Special Views/Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[[Knee Joint]]** | • AP<br>• Lateral | **Weight-bearing AP:** To assess joint space narrowing in osteoarthritis. |
| **[[Patella]]** | • AP<br>• Lateral | **Skyline / Sunrise / Merchant View:** Essential to view the patellofemoral joint space and vertical fractures of the patella not seen on AP. |
| **[[Tibial Plateau]]** | • AP<br>• Lateral | **Intercondylar (Tunnel) View:** Visualizes loose bodies, tibial spine avulsions, and osteochondritis dissecans in the notch. |

#### 3. Ankle and Foot
| Region | Standard Views | Special Views/Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[[Ankle Joint]]** | • AP<br>• Lateral<br>• Mortise View | **[[Mortise View]]**: Leg internally rotated 15-20°. This eliminates the overlap of the fibula and tibia, allowing assessment of the talar dome and joint symmetry (syndesmosis injury). |
| **[[Calcaneum]]** | • Lateral<br>• Axial | **Axial (Harris) View:** Shows the body of the calcaneum, tuberosity, and sustentaculum tali. Essential for widening of the heel bone. |
| **[[Foot]]** | • Dorsoplantar (AP)<br>• Oblique<br>• Lateral | **Oblique View:** Best for visualizing fractures of the metatarsals and tarsal bones (e.g., Lisfranc injury). |

**Mnemonic for Ankle Views:**
**ALM** for Ankle: **A**nteroposterior, **L**ateral, **M**ortise.
