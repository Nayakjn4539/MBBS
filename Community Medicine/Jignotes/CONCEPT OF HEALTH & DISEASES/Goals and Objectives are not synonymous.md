# [[CONCEPT OF HEALTH & DISEASES]] > Goals and Objectives are not synonymous

# Distinction Between Goals and Objectives in Health Planning

In the context of **[[Health Planning]]** and management, the terms "Goal" and "Objective" are frequently confused, but they represent distinct concepts regarding specificity, time, and measurability. A third term, "Target," is often used in conjunction with them.

### 1. Goal
A **Goal** is defined as the **ultimate desired state** towards which objectives and resources are directed.
*   **Characteristics:**
    *   It is generally broad and formulated at the highest level.
    *   It is **not constrained** by time or existing resources.
    *    It is not necessarily attainable in the short term; it serves as a vision or a direction.
*   **Example:** "[[Health for All]] by the year 2000" was a goal. Another example is "To improve the quality of life of the people."

### 2. Objective
An **Objective** is defined as a **planned end-point** of all activities.
*   **Characteristics:**
    *   It is **precise** and specific.
    *   It represents a measurable step towards attaining the goal.
    *   It is concerned directly with the **problem** itself.
    *   Objectives are usually time-bound.
*   **Example:** "To reduce the [[Infant Mortality Rate]] (IMR) to below 30 per 1000 live births by the year 2010."

### 3. Target
While the question focuses on Goals vs. Objectives, **Targets** are integral to this hierarchy. A target refers to a **discrete activity** (e.g., number of vaccinations done, number of vasectomies performed).
*   It measures the **degree of achievement**.
*   It is concerned with the **factors** involved in a problem rather than the problem itself.

---

### Comparison: Goals vs. Objectives

| Feature | **GOAL** | **OBJECTIVE** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Definition** | The ultimate desired state or vision. | The planned end-point of activities. |
| **Nature** | Broad, general, and abstract. | Precise, specific, and concrete. |
| **Time Frame** | Long-term; often not strictly time-bound. | Short to medium-term; time-bound. |
| **Constraints** | Not constrained by current resources. | Based on available resources and feasibility. |
| **Focus** | The final destination (Vision). | The specific steps to get there (Action). |
| **Attainability** | Not necessarily fully attainable immediately. | Must be attainable and realistic. |

---

### The SMART Criteria for Objectives
To ensure an objective is well-formulated and distinct from a vague goal, it must satisfy the **SMART** criteria:

1.  **S - Specific:** Clear and precise outcome (e.g., "Reduce TB prevalence," not just "Improve health").
2.  **M - Measurable:** Can be quantified (e.g., "By 10%"). It answers "How much?".
3.  **A - Attainable:** Can be achieved with available resources.
4.  **R - Relevant/Result-oriented:** Aligned with the organization's goals.
5.  **T - Time-bound:** Has a specific deadline (e.g., "By 2025").

---

### Clinical & Programmatic Relevance
Understanding this distinction is vital for **[[National Health Programmes]]**:
*   **In the [[National Tuberculosis Elimination Program]] (NTEP):**
    *   **Goal:** To eliminate Tuberculosis from India.
    *   **Objective:** To achieve a cure rate of at least 85% among newly detected infectious cases.
    *   **Target:** To collect 100 sputum samples per month in a specific PHC.

Without precise objectives, a program cannot be evaluated for **efficiency** or **effectiveness**.
