# [[Oral Cavity]] > Benign lesions of Oral cavity

### Classification of Benign Lesions of the Oral Cavity

Benign lesions of the oral cavity can be broadly classified based on their consistency (solid vs. cystic) and origin (epithelial, connective tissue, or developmental).

| **Category** | **Lesions** |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Solid Tumors** | [[Squamous Papilloma]], [[Fibroma]], [[Hemangioma]], [[Lymphangioma]], [[Granular Cell Tumor]], [[Pyogenic Granuloma]], [[Torus]], [[Ameloblastoma]], [[Pleomorphic Adenoma]] |
| **Cystic Lesions** | [[Mucocele]], [[Ranula]], [[Dermoid Cyst]] |
| **Premalignant Lesions** | [[Leukoplakia]], [[Erythroplakia]], [[Oral Submucous Fibrosis]], [[Lichen Planus]] |
| **Miscellaneous** | [[Geographic Tongue]], [[Median Rhomboid Glossitis]], [[Fordyce Spots]], [[Hairy Tongue]] |

### Solid Benign Tumors

#### 1. Squamous Papilloma
*   **Etiology:** Usually associated with **Human Papillomavirus ([[HPV]])** subtypes 6 and 11.
*   **Clinical Features:**
    *   Common in 3rd to 5th decades.
    *   **Site:** Soft/Hard palate, uvula, tongue, lips.
    *   **Appearance:** Pedunculated, white, warty (verrucous) surface (cauliflower-like). Usually < 1 cm.
*   **Treatment:** Surgical excision or ablation with [[CO2 Laser]]. Recurrence is rare.

#### 2. Fibroma (Irritation Fibroma/Fibroepithelial Polyp)
*   **Etiology:** Reactive hyperplasia due to chronic irritation (e.g., cheek bite, sharp tooth) rather than a true neoplasm.
*   **Clinical Features:**
    *   **Site:** Buccal mucosa (along the bite line), tongue, labial mucosa.
    *   **Appearance:** Smooth, pink (mucosa-covered), sessile or pedunculated, firm consistency.
*   **Treatment:** Conservative surgical excision.

#### 3. Hemangioma
*   **Types:** Capillary (more common) or Cavernous.
*   **Clinical Features:**
    *   Mostly seen in children; congenital lesions often regress.
    *   **Site:** Lips, tongue, buccal mucosa.
    *   **Appearance:** Soft, painless, red or blue mass. Blanches on pressure.
    *   **Phlebectasia:** Dilated veins seen in older adults (caviar tongue).
*   **Treatment:**
    *   Observation (for spontaneous involution in children).
    *   Active treatment for persistent/growing lesions: [[Microembolization]], Cryotherapy, Laser, or Sclerotherapy.

#### 4. Lymphangioma
*   **Clinical Features:**
    *   **Site:** Anterior 2/3rd of the [[Tongue]] (most common).
    *   **Appearance:** Can cause diffuse macroglossia or localized "frog-spawn" appearance (pebbly surface). Soft and compressible.
*   **Treatment:** Surgical excision. Complete excision is difficult; partial excision is done to reduce bulk.

#### 5. Pyogenic Granuloma
*   **Etiology:** Reactive inflammatory hyperplasia in response to trauma or hormonal changes (e.g., **Pregnancy Tumor** or [[Pregnancy Granuloma]]).
*   **Clinical Features:**
    *   **Site:** Anterior [[Gingiva]] (most common), lips, tongue.
    *   **Appearance:** Soft, reddish-purple, pedunculated/sessile, **bleeds easily** on touch.
*   **Treatment:** Surgical excision. Recurrence is rare if excised completely.

#### 6. Torus (Exostosis)
*   **Definition:** Non-neoplastic bony outgrowth.
*   **Types:**
    *   **[[Torus Palatinus]]:** Midline of the hard palate.
    *   **[[Torus Mandibularis]]:** Lingual surface of mandible (premolar region), usually bilateral.
*   **Clinical Implications:** Usually innocuous. Removal indicated only if they interfere with denture fitting or speech.

#### 7. Granular Cell Tumor (Myoblastoma)
*   **Origin:** Derived from **Schwann cells** (neural origin).
*   **Site:** Tongue (most common).
*   **Appearance:** Firm, submucosal nodule.
*   **Histology:** Overlying epithelium shows **pseudoepitheliomatous hyperplasia** (can be mistaken for SCC).
*   **Treatment:** Excision.

### Cystic Lesions

#### 1. Mucocele
*   **Definition:** Retention cyst of minor salivary glands.
*   **Etiology:** Trauma to the duct (extravasation type) or duct obstruction (retention type).
*   **Clinical Features:**
    *   **Site:** **Lower Lip** is the most common site.
    *   **Appearance:** Soft, cystic, bluish, transparent swelling.
*   **Treatment:** Surgical excision of the cyst along with the underlying minor salivary gland to prevent recurrence.

#### 2. Ranula
*   **Definition:** Cystic lesion in the floor of the mouth arising from the **[[Sublingual Salivary Gland]]**.
*   **Appearance:** Unilateral, bluish, translucent swelling resembling a **frog's belly**.
*   **Types:**
    1.  **Simple Ranula:** Confined to the floor of the mouth (above mylohyoid).
    2.  **[[Plunging Ranula]]:** Herniates through the mylohyoid muscle into the neck (submandibular/submental space).
*   **Treatment:**
    *   Small: Excision.
    *   Large/Plunging: Excision of the cyst **along with the sublingual gland** (to prevent recurrence). Marsupialization has high recurrence rates.

#### 3. Dermoid Cyst
*   **Pathology:** Developmental cyst containing skin appendages (hair, sweat glands).
*   **Clinical Features:**
    *   **Sublingual Dermoid:** Above mylohyoid; pushes tongue up. Median or lateral.
    *   **Submental Dermoid:** Below mylohyoid; presents as double chin.
    *   **Differentiating feature:** Opaque/white mass (unlike translucent Ranula) and doughy consistency.
*   **Treatment:** Surgical excision.

### Premalignant Lesions (Potentially Malignant Disorders)

#### 1. Leukoplakia
*   **Definition:** A white patch that cannot be rubbed off and cannot be characterized clinically or pathologically as any other disease. Diagnosis of exclusion.
*   **Etiology:** Tobacco (smoking/chewing), Alcohol, Friction.
*   **Types:**
    *   **Homogenous:** Smooth/wrinkled white patch (lower risk).
    *   **Non-homogenous (Speckled/Erosive/Nodular):** High risk of malignancy.
    *   **Proliferative Verrucous Leukoplakia:** High risk, resistant to treatment.
*   **Malignant Potential:** 1â€“17.5% (Avg 5%). Higher risk if on **floor of mouth** or **ventral tongue**.
*   **Management:** Biopsy to rule out dysplasia/carcinoma. Cessation of habits. Surgical excision/Laser ablation for dysplastic lesions.

#### 2. Erythroplakia
*   **Definition:** A red velvety patch that cannot be characterized as another disease.
*   **Significance:** Much higher malignant potential than leukoplakia (severe dysplasia or Carcinoma in situ often present at diagnosis).
*   **Management:** Excision biopsy.

#### 3. Oral Submucous Fibrosis (OSMF)
*   **Etiology:** Chronic chewing of **Areca nut (Betel nut)** containing arecoline.
*   **Pathology:** Fibroelastic transformation of connective tissue.
*   **Clinical Features:**
    *   Burning sensation on eating spicy food (early sign).
    *   **Trismus** (progressive inability to open mouth) due to fibrous bands.
    *   Blanched, marble-like mucosa.
    *   Stiff tongue and soft palate.
*   **Treatment:** Stop habit. Intralesional steroids/hyaluronidase. Surgical release for severe trismus.

#### 4. Lichen Planus
*   **Etiology:** Autoimmune T-cell mediated.
*   **Forms:**
    *   **Reticular:** Wickham's striae (lace-like pattern), asymptomatic.
    *   **Erosive:** Painful ulcers, potential for malignancy.
*   **Treatment:** Topical steroids for erosive form.

### Miscellaneous & Tumor-like Conditions

| **Condition** | **Description** |
| :--- | :--- |
| **[[Geographic Tongue]]** | Migratory glossitis. Erythematous patches with white borders. Map-like appearance. Asymptomatic. |
| **[[Median Rhomboid Glossitis]]** | Red depapillated patch in midline dorsum of tongue (anterior to circumvallate papillae). Associated with *Candida*. |
| **[[Fordyce Spots]]** | Ectopic **sebaceous glands**. Yellowish granules on buccal mucosa/lips. Normal variant. |
| **[[Hairy Tongue]]** | Hypertrophy of filiform papillae. Stained brown/black (tobacco/chromogenic bacteria). |
| **[[Torus Palatinus]]** | Bony exostosis in the midline of hard palate. |

### Clinical Implications
*   **The Rule of Biopsy:** Any ulcer or lesion in the oral cavity persisting for more than **3 weeks** despite conservative treatment mandates a biopsy to rule out malignancy.
*   **Danger Areas:** Leukoplakia on the **floor of the mouth** and **ventral surface of the tongue** has the highest risk of malignant transformation ("Coffin corner" of the mouth).
*   **Plunging Ranula:** Always differentiate a submandibular neck swelling from a plunging ranula or lymphadenopathy. A ranula is translucent and non-tender unless infected.

**Mnemonic for Premalignant Lesions:** **LES**s **P**robable to heal
*   **L**eukoplakia
*   **E**rythroplakia (Highest risk)
*   **S**ubmucous Fibrosis
*   **P**lanus (Lichen Planus - erosive type)
