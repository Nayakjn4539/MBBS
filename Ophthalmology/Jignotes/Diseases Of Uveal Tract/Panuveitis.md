# [[Panuveitis]]

### Definition and Classification
**[[Panuveitis]]** is the generalized inflammation of the entire [[uveal tract]], involving the [[iris]], [[ciliary body]], and [[choroid]] simultaneously. It is a form of diffuse uveitis where no single portion of the uvea is the predominant site of inflammation.

**Anatomical Classification:**
*   **Anterior Uveitis:** Iris and pars plicata.
*   **Intermediate Uveitis:** Pars plana and peripheral retina.
*   **Posterior Uveitis:** Choroid (often involving retina).
*   **Panuveitis:** Inflammation of the whole uvea.

### Etiology
Causes of panuveitis can be broadly categorized into infectious and non-infectious etiologies.

| Category | Specific Causes |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Infectious** | • [[Tuberculosis]]<br>• [[Syphilis]]<br>• [[Toxoplasmosis]]<br>• [[Leprosy]]<br>• Lyme disease |
| **Non-Infectious (Immune-Mediated)** | • [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada Syndrome]] (VKH)<br>• [[Sympathetic Ophthalmitis]]<br>• [[Sarcoidosis]]<br>• [[Behcet's Disease]] |
| **Masquerade Syndromes** | • [[Intraocular lymphoma]]<br>• [[Retinoblastoma]]<br>• [[Leukemia]] |

### Specific Clinical Entities

#### 1. [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada Syndrome]] (VKH)
A bilateral, granulomatous panuveitis affecting multiple systems (Eye, Ear, Skin, Meninges).
*   **Pathology:** Autoimmune response against melanocytes.
*   **Systemic Features:**
    *   **Neurological:** Meningismus, encephalopathy.
    *   **Cutaneous:** [[Alopecia]], [[Poliosis]] (whitening of eyelashes), [[Vitiligo]].
    *   **Auditory:** Tinnitus, vertigo, deafness.
*   **Ocular Features:**
    *   Chronic granulomatous anterior and posterior uveitis.
    *   **Exudative [[Retinal Detachment]]**.
    *   **[[Sunset Glow Fundus]]:** RPE atrophy giving a reddish-orange appearance.
    *   **[[Sugiura Sign]]:** Perilimbal vitiligo.

#### 2. [[Sympathetic Ophthalmitis]]
A rare, bilateral, granulomatous panuveitis following penetrating ocular trauma or surgery.
*   **Mechanism:** Trauma to one eye (Exciting eye) leads to autoimmune reaction against uveal pigment, causing inflammation in the fellow eye (Sympathizing eye).
*   **Timing:**
    *   Usually occurs 4–8 weeks after injury.
    *   Almost never occurs before 2 weeks (safe period).
*   **Clinical Features:**
    *   **Prodromal:** Blurring of near vision (due to loss of accommodation) and photophobia in the sound eye.
    *   **Signs:** [[Retrolental flare]], Mutton-fat [[Keratic Precipitates]] (KPs), Ciliary congestion.
*   **Pathology:**
    *   **[[Dalen-Fuchs Nodules]]:** Nodular aggregations of epithelioid cells and lymphocytes located between [[Retinal Pigment Epithelium]] (RPE) and [[Bruch's membrane]].
*   **Prophylaxis:** Early [[Enucleation]] of the injured eye (within 2 weeks) is the best prevention. **Evisceration** is contraindicated as residual uveal tissue can incite the reaction.

#### 3. [[Behcet's Disease]]
An idiopathic multisystem disorder associated with **[[HLA-B5]]** and **[[HLA-B51]]**.
*   **Classic Triad:** Recurrent oral aphthous ulcers, genital ulcers, and uveitis.
*   **Ocular Features:**
    *   Recurrent, bilateral, non-granulomatous panuveitis.
    *   **[[Hypopyon]]:** Often described as a "cold hypopyon" (minimal fibrin, shifts with head position).
    *   **Retinitis:** Necrotic infiltrates, diffuse vascular leakage, and periphlebitis.

#### 4. [[Sarcoidosis]]
A chronic multisystem granulomatous disorder.
*   **Ocular Features:**
    *   Granulomatous panuveitis with "Mutton-fat" KPs.
    *   **Candle-wax drippings:** Periphlebitis retinae with sheathing.
    *   Choroidal and retinal granulomas.
*   **[[Heerfordt's Syndrome]] (Uveoparotid Fever):**
    *   Bilateral granulomatous panuveitis.
    *   Painful enlargement of [[Parotid glands]].
    *   Cranial nerve palsies (especially [[Facial nerve palsy]]).

### Comparison: Panuveitis vs. Panophthalmitis vs. Endophthalmitis

It is crucial to differentiate Panuveitis (inflammation of the uveal tract) from purulent infections of the globe.

| Feature | [[Endophthalmitis]] | [[Panophthalmitis]] |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Definition** | Suppurative inflammation of internal structures (uvea, retina, vitreous). | Intense purulent inflammation of the **whole eyeball** including **[[Tenon's Capsule]]**. |
| **Extent** | Involves all layers **except sclera**. | Involves all layers **including sclera and Tenon's capsule**. |
| **Causes** | Post-op infection (e.g., *Staph epidermidis*, *P. acnes*), Trauma. | Progression of endophthalmitis, severe trauma. |
| **Ocular Movement** | Usually normal or minimally restricted. | **Restricted and painful** (due to Tenon's involvement). |
| **Proptosis** | Absent. | **Slight Proptosis** present. |
| **Treatment** | Intravitreal antibiotics, [[Vitrectomy]]. | **[[Evisceration]]** (Enucleation is contraindicated due to risk of meningitis). |

### Management of Panuveitis

**1. General Measures:**
*   **Cycloplegics:** [[Atropine]] (1%) to relieve pain, prevent posterior synechiae, and give rest to the ciliary muscle.
*   **Dark Goggles:** To reduce photophobia.

**2. Anti-inflammatory Therapy:**
*   **Corticosteroids:** The mainstay of treatment. Administered topically, periocularly (sub-tenon injections), and systemically depending on severity.
*   **Immunosuppressives:** Used for steroid-resistant cases, particularly in VKH, Sympathetic Ophthalmitis, and Behcet's. Agents include [[Methotrexate]], [[Azathioprine]], [[Cyclosporine]], and [[Cyclophosphamide]].

**3. Specific Therapy:**
*   Treat the underlying cause (e.g., Antitubercular drugs for TB, Penicillin for Syphilis).
