# [[Diseases Of Cornea]] > Viral Keratitis

# Viral Keratitis

Viral infections are a major cause of corneal morbidity. The two most clinically significant viral infections affecting the cornea are **Herpes Simplex** and **Herpes Zoster**.

---

## 1. Herpes Simplex Keratitis (HSV)

HSV is a double-stranded DNA virus and is the most common cause of corneal blindness in developed countries.

### Etiology and Pathogenesis
*   **Types:**
    *   **HSV-1:** Infection usually "above the waist" (face, lips, eyes). Most ocular infections are HSV-1.
    *   **HSV-2:** Infection "below the waist" (genital). Transmitted to neonates through the birth canal.
*   **Mode of Infection:** Direct contact.
*   **Latency:** After primary infection, the virus travels up the sensory nerve and lies dormant in the [[trigeminal ganglion]].
*   **Reactivation Triggers:** Fever ("fever blister"), UV exposure, stress, menstruation, trauma, and **topical/systemic steroids**.

### Clinical Classification & Features

#### A. Primary Ocular Herpes
*   Occurs in non-immune hosts (usually children 6 months - 5 years).
*   **Features:**
    *   Vesicular [[blepharitis]].
    *   Acute follicular [[conjunctivitis]] with regional lymphadenopathy.
    *   **Keratitis:** Usually fine/coarse epithelial punctate keratitis; rarely dendritic.
*   Usually self-limiting.

#### B. Recurrent Ocular Herpes (Secondary)
This is the more common, sight-threatening form.

**1. Epithelial Keratitis**
*   **Pathology:** Active viral replication in epithelial cells.
*   **Symptoms:** Redness, photophobia, watering, blurred vision.
*   **Signs:**
    *   **Punctate Epithelial Keratitis:** Initial fine/coarse spots.
    *   **Dendritic Ulcer (Classic):**
        *   Linear, branching, zig-zag ulcer.
        *   **Terminal bulbs/knobs:** Ends of branches are knobbed.
        *   **Staining:** Floor stains with **Fluorescein** (true ulcer); virus-laden margins stain with **Rose Bengal**.
        *   **Sensation:** Marked diminution of [[corneal sensation]] (Hypoesthesia).
    *   **Geographic (Amoeboid) Ulcer:** Large, irregular ulcer formed by coalescing dendrites. Often caused by inappropriate use of **topical steroids** in dendritic ulcers.

**2. Stromal Keratitis**
*   **Disciform Keratitis (Endotheliitis):**
    *   **Mechanism:** Type IV Hypersensitivity (Delayed) to viral antigen.
    *   **Signs:** Focal, disc-shaped central [[stromal edema]] without necrosis. Intact epithelium. Folds in [[Descemet's membrane]].
    *   **Wessely Immune Ring:** A ring of stromal infiltrate surrounding the edema (Antigen-Antibody complex).
*   **Necrotizing Stromal Keratitis:**
    *   **Mechanism:** Active viral invasion and tissue destruction.
    *   **Signs:** Cheesy white necrotic infiltrates, vascularization, and abscess formation.

**3. Metaherpetic (Trophic/Neurotrophic) Keratitis**
*   **Mechanism:** Not active viral replication. Mechanical healing defect due to basement membrane damage and denervation (neurotrophic).
*   **Signs:** Indolent, oval ulcer with smooth, grey, thickened epithelial borders (heaped up epithelium). No branching.

### Laboratory Diagnosis
*   **PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction):** Most reliable/sensitive.
*   **Giemsa Stain:** Shows multinucleated giant cells and eosinophilic intranuclear inclusion bodies (**Lipschutz bodies**).
*   **Viral Culture:** Definitive but slow.

### Treatment

| Type of Keratitis | Management |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Epithelial (Dendritic)** | • **Antivirals:** [[Acyclovir]] 3% ointment (5 times/day for 14-21 days).<br>• Alternatives: Ganciclovir gel, Trifluridine drops.<br>• **Debridement:** Removal of virus-laden cells.<br>• **Contraindication:** **Topical Steroids** (Risk of geographic ulcer/perforation). |
| **Stromal (Disciform)** | • **Topical Steroids:** To control hypersensitivity (prednisolone, tapered slowly).<br>• **Antiviral Cover:** Essential (e.g., Acyclovir ointment BD/TDS) to prevent epithelial recurrence while on steroids. |
| **Metaherpetic (Trophic)** | • **Lubricants:** Preservative-free artificial tears.<br>• **Bandage Contact Lens.**<br>• **Tarsorrhaphy:** Lateral lid closure to promote healing. |

## 2. Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)

An acute infection of the Gasserian ganglion of the [[trigeminal nerve]] by the **Varicella-Zoster Virus (VZV)**.

### Etiology
*   Reactivation of latent VZV (chickenpox virus) in the sensory ganglion.
*   **Risk Factors:** Elderly (>60 years), immunocompromised status (HIV/AIDS, malignancy, prolonged steroid use).

### Clinical Features
*   **Prodrome:** Fever, malaise, severe neuralgic pain along the nerve distribution.
*   **Rash:** Unilateral vesicular eruption strictly limited to one side of the midline (dermatomal).
*   **Nerve Involvement:** Frontal nerve (most frequent).
*   **[[Hutchinson's Sign]]:** Vesicles on the tip/side of the nose (involvement of **nasociliary nerve**). Indicates high risk of ocular involvement (approx. 50-75%).

> [!warning] Diagram Alert
> Hutchinson's sign showing vesicles on the tip of the nose

### Ocular Manifestations (Zoster Keratitis)
Occurs in 40% of cases.

1.  **Epithelial Keratitis:**
    *   **Punctate Epithelial Keratitis:** Early fine/coarse spots.
    *   **Pseudodendrites (Microdendritic):**
        *   Peripheral, stellate, or linear lesions.
        *   **Tapered ends** (No terminal knobs).
        *   Raised/painted-on appearance (mucous plaques).
        *   Stain poorly with fluorescein but well with Rose Bengal.
2.  **Nummular Keratitis:** Multiple, granular, coin-shaped anterior stromal infiltrates surrounded by a halo of haze.
3.  **Disciform Keratitis:** Similar to HSV but usually preceded by nummular keratitis.
4.  **Mucous Plaque Keratitis:** Elevated mucous plaques occurring months after onset.
5.  **Neuroparalytic Ulcer:** Due to anesthesia dolorosa (painful anesthesia).

### Other Ocular Complications
*   **Uveitis:** Granulomatous iridocyclitis (often with hyphema - acute hemorrhagic uveitis).
*   **Glaucoma:** Secondary to trabeculitis.
*   **Scleritis/Episcleritis**.
*   **Acute Retinal Necrosis (ARN)**.
*   **Post-Herpetic Neuralgia (PHN):** Chronic pain persisting after rash resolution.

### Comparison: Dendritic Ulcer (HSV) vs. Pseudodendrite (HZO)

| Feature | HSV Dendritic Ulcer | HZO Pseudodendrite |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Shape** | Linear, branching | Stellate, stuck-on plaque |
| **Ends** | **Knobbed/Bulbous** | **Tapered** |
| **Ulceration** | True ulcer (excavation) | Minimal/Raised mucous plaque |
| **Fluorescein** | Stains brilliantly | Stains poorly/negative |
| **Rose Bengal** | Stains margins | Stains the whole lesion |

### Treatment

**A. Systemic Therapy** (Critical to start within 72 hours of rash)
*   **Oral Antivirals:**
    *   **[[Acyclovir]]**: **800 mg 5 times/day** for 7-10 days.
    *   Valacyclovir: 1000 mg TDS.
    *   Famciclovir: 500 mg TDS.
*   **Analgesics:** For acute pain.
*   **Systemic Steroids:** For neurological complications (e.g., optic neuritis) or severe proptosis.

**B. Local Ocular Therapy**
*   **Keratitis/Uveitis:** Topical steroids (e.g., Prednisolone) + Cycloplegics (Atropine).
*   **Neurotrophic Ulcer:** Lubricants, tarsorrhaphy.
*   **Secondary Infection:** Topical antibiotics.

**C. Post-Herpetic Neuralgia (PHN)**
*   Amitriptyline (TCA), Gabapentin, Capsaicin cream.
