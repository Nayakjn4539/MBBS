# [[Diseases Of Conjunctiva]] > Conjunctival Disorders table

### **Comprehensive Guide to Conjunctival Disorders**

#### **1. Infectious Conjunctivitis**

The following table details conjunctival disorders caused by bacteria, viruses, and chlamydia.

| Disease | Defect / Clinical Features | Etiopathogenesis | Treatment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Acute Mucopurulent Conjunctivitis** | • **Redness**, gritty sensation, mucopurulent discharge (sticking of lids in morning),<br>• **Papillae** and superficial congestion,<br>• Colored halos may be present due to mucus | • **Staphylococcus aureus** (m/c worldwide),,<br>• *Streptococcus pneumoniae* (Petechial hemorrhage)<br>• *Haemophilus aegyptius* (Koch-Weeks bacillus) | • Topical **Broad-spectrum antibiotics** (Chloramphenicol, Moxifloxacin, Tobramycin),<br>• Irrigation with saline<br>• **No Bandaging** (prevents discharge exit, increases temp) |
| **Hyperacute (Purulent) Conjunctivitis** | • **Copious purulent discharge**,<br>• Severe lid swelling/chemosis<br>• Pre-auricular lymphadenopathy,<br>• Risk of corneal perforation, | • **Neisseria gonorrhoeae**,<br>• *Neisseria meningitidis*<br>• Transmitted via genital-hand-eye contact | • **Systemic**: IM Ceftriaxone (1g stat)<br>• **Topical**: Ciprofloxacin/Gentamicin drops frequently<br>• Frequent saline irrigation<br>• Treat sexual partner |
| **Angular Conjunctivitis** | • Excoriation of skin at **canthi** (angles),<br>• Redness at intermarginal strip<br>• Associated blepharitis | • **Moraxella axenfeld** (diplobacillus)<br>• Produces proteolytic enzymes that macerate epithelium<br>• *Staph. aureus* (rarely) | • **Zinc boric drops** (inhibits proteolytic ferment),<br>• Oxytetracycline ointment<br>• Treat nasal carrier state |
| **Membranous Conjunctivitis** | • **True Membrane**: Bleeds on peeling,<br>• Swollen, hard lids<br>• Systemic toxicity possible | • **Corynebacterium diphtheriae**,<br>• *Streptococcus haemolyticus* (virulent) | • **Systemic Penicillin**<br>• **Anti-diphtheritic serum** (ADS)<br>• Isolation of patient |
| **Pseudomembranous Conjunctivitis** | • **Pseudomembrane**: Coagulated exudate, peels easily **without bleeding**, | • Adenovirus (Severe)<br>• *Strep. pyogenes*, *N. gonorrhoeae*, *C. diphtheriae* (low virulence), | • Removal of membrane<br>• Topical antibiotics/steroids depending on cause |
| **Trachoma** (Egyptian Ophthalmia) | • **Sago grain follicles** (upper tarsus),<br>• **Arlt’s Line**: Linear scarring,<br>• **Herbert’s Pits**: Limbal depressions (pathognomonic),<br>• **Pannus**: Corneal vascularization | • **Chlamydia trachomatis** (Serotypes **A, B, Ba, C**),<br>• Spread: Finger, Flies, Fomites (3Fs)<br>• Chronic cicatrizing inflammation | • **SAFE Strategy**: **S**urgery (for trichiasis), **A**ntibiotics, **F**acial cleanliness, **E**nvironmental change<br>• **Drug**: Azithromycin (1g single dose) or Tetracycline ointment |
| **Adult Inclusion Conjunctivitis** | • Acute follicular conjunctivitis (Lower lid predominant)<br>• Pre-auricular lymphadenopathy | • **Chlamydia trachomatis** (Serotypes **D to K**),<br>• Sexual transmission (oculogenital)<br>• "Swimming pool conjunctivitis" | • Oral **Azithromycin** (1g) or Doxycycline,<br>• Topical Tetracycline<br>• Treat sexual partner |
| **Adenoviral Conjunctivitis** (EKC & PCF) | • **Follicular** reaction<br>• Watery discharge<br>• **EKC**: Epidemic Keratoconjunctivitis (severe, corneal involvement)<br>• **PCF**: Pharyngoconjunctival Fever (Fever + Pharyngitis), | • **Adenovirus**<br>• EKC: Serotypes 8, 19, 37,<br>• PCF: Serotypes 3, 4, 7, | • Self-limiting; Supportive care (cool compress, lubricants)<br>• Topical steroids only for severe membrane/corneal infiltrates<br>• Strict hygiene (highly contagious) |
| **Acute Hemorrhagic Conjunctivitis** (Apollo) | • **Subconjunctival Hemorrhage** (Frank blood under conjunctiva)<br>• Follicles, swelling | • **Enterovirus 70**,<br>• **Coxsackie A24**,<br>• Picornavirus group | • Self-resolving (5-7 days)<br>• Prophylactic antibiotics for secondary infection |
| **Molluscum Contagiosum** | • **Umbilicated nodule** on lid margin<br>• Chronic follicular conjunctivitis due to viral shedding into sac | • **Poxvirus**,<br>• Toxic reaction to viral debris | • Surgical excision of the nodule, |

**Clinical Implication:**
*   **Hyperacute Conjunctivitis** requires immediate attention to prevent corneal melting and perforation.
*   **Trachoma** is a leading cause of preventable blindness; always look for **Arlt's line** (scarring) and **Herbert's pits** (limbal follicles).

#### **2. Allergic and Immunological Disorders**

These are primarily hypersensitivity reactions characterized by itching and specific morphological signs.

| Disease | Defect / Clinical Features | Etiopathogenesis | Treatment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Simple Allergic Conjunctivitis** (Hay Fever) | • **Itching**, watery discharge, chemosis,<br>• Mild papillary reaction | • **Type I Hypersensitivity** (IgE mediated)<br>• Pollens, dust, animal dander | • Mast cell stabilizers (Sodium Cromoglycate)<br>• Antihistamines (Olopatadine)<br>• Avoid allergens |
| **Vernal Keratoconjunctivitis (VKC)** / Spring Catarrh | • **Cobblestone Papillae** (Palpebral form),<br>• **Horner-Trantas Spots** (Limbal form - eosinophils),<br>• **Ropy discharge** (Maxwell-Lyon sign),<br>• **Shield Ulcer** on cornea, | • **Type I Hypersensitivity** (IgE) + Type IV,<br>• Young boys (5-15 yrs), hot/dry climate,<br>• Recurrent/Seasonal | • **Topical Steroids** (for acute episodes),<br>• Mast cell stabilizers (maintenance)<br>• Topical Cyclosporine<br>• **Supratarsal steroid injection** for refractory cases |
| **Atopic Keratoconjunctivitis (AKC)** | • **Chronic**, perennial (year-round)<br>• **Hertoghe sign** (loss of lateral eyebrow)<br>• Lid thickening (tylosis), skin fissures | • Type I & IV Hypersensitivity<br>• Associated with **atopic dermatitis** / asthma,<br>• Affects adults more than children | • Similar to VKC but requires prolonged therapy<br>• Treat associated dermatitis<br>• Systemic antihistamines |
| **Giant Papillary Conjunctivitis (GPC)** | • **Giant Papillae** (>1mm or >0.3mm clinically) on upper tarsus,<br>• Mucus discharge, itching | • Mechanical irritation + Immune reaction<br>• **Contact Lenses** (soft), prosthesis, exposed sutures, | • **Discontinue Contact Lens** wear,<br>• Mast cell stabilizers<br>• Remove sutures/polish prosthesis |
| **Phlyctenular Conjunctivitis** | • **Phlycten**: Pinkish-white nodule near limbus,<br>• Hyperemia localized to nodule<br>• Can cause fascicular ulcer (migrating to cornea) | • **Type IV Hypersensitivity** (Delayed),<br>• Endogenous antigens: **Tuberculosis** (India), **Staphylococcus** (West), | • **Topical Steroids**,<br>• Treat underlying cause (TB/Blepharitis) |
| **Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)** | • Acute mucocutaneous blistering<br>• Pseudomembranes, **Symblepharon** (adhesions)<br>• Dry eye, trichiasis | • **Type III / IV Hypersensitivity**<br>• Triggered by drugs (**Sulphonamides**, anticonvulsants) or infections, | • **Acute**: Lubricants, lysis of adhesions (glass rod), topical steroids/antibiotics<br>• **Chronic**: Mucous membrane grafting, keratoprosthesis |
| **Ocular Mucous Membrane Pemphigoid (OMMP)** | • Progressive conjunctival shrinkage<br>• **Symblepharon** (inferior fornix shortening),<br>• Ankyloblepharon, Dry eye | • **Type II Hypersensitivity**<br>• Autoantibodies against basement membrane zone | • Systemic immunosuppression (Dapsone, Cyclophosphamide),<br>• Lubricants |

**Clinical Implication:**
*   **VKC** presents with intense itching and ropy discharge in young boys.
*   **Phlyctenulosis** is often a marker for Tuberculosis in developing countries.
*   **SJS/OMMP** can lead to blinding sequelae like symblepharon and severe dry eye; early intervention to prevent adhesions is critical.

#### **3. Ophthalmia Neonatorum (Neonatal Conjunctivitis)**

Conjunctivitis occurring within the first **28 days (4 weeks)** of life.

| Etiology | Onset | Features | Treatment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Chemical** | < 24 hours | Mild hyperemia, watery discharge. Due to Silver Nitrate (Crede's method), | Self-limiting; Stop irritant |
| **Gonococcal** (*N. gonorrhoeae*) | 24-48 hours | **Hyperacute**, severe purulent discharge, chemosis. Risk of corneal melt, | Systemic Ceftriaxone/Cefotaxime + Saline irrigation, |
| **Bacterial** (*Staph, Strep*) | 4-5 days | Mucopurulent discharge | Broad-spectrum topical antibiotics (Erythromycin/Bacitracin) |
| **Herpes Simplex** (HSV-2) | 5-7 days | Vesicular rash, keratitis, serous discharge | Systemic & Topical Acyclovir |
| **Chlamydial** (*C. trachomatis* D-K) | 1-2 weeks | **Most common cause**. Mucopurulent discharge. May cause pneumonia, | Systemic **Erythromycin** syrup (treats pneumonia risk) + Topical Erythromycin |

#### **4. Degenerative and Symptomatic Conditions**

| Disease | Defect / Clinical Features | Etiopathogenesis | Treatment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pterygium** | • **Triangular fibrovascular growth** encroaching onto cornea (usually nasal),<br>• **Stocker’s Line**: Iron deposition at head,<br>• Astigmatism, visual loss if covers pupil | • Elastotic degeneration due to **UV exposure** (sunlight), dust, dry heat,<br>• Destruction of Bowman's layer | • **Excision with Conjunctival Autograft (CAG)** (Gold standard to prevent recurrence),<br>• Mitomycin C (MMC) to reduce recurrence |
| **Pinguecula** | • Yellowish-white nodule on bulbar conjunctiva near limbus,<br>• Does not cross limbus | • Elastotic degeneration (UV/Sun exposure)<br>• Hyaline deposition | • No treatment usually<br>• Lubricants if irritated<br>• Steroids if inflamed (Pingueculitis) |
| **Concretions** (Lithiasis) | • Yellowish-white hard spots on palpebral conjunctiva,<br>• Foreign body sensation | • Accumulation of inspissated mucus and epithelial debris in glands of Henle,<br>• Seen in elderly or chronic trachoma | • Removal with hypodermic needle, |
| **Xerosis** (Dry Eye) | • **Bitot’s Spots**: Foamy triangular spots (Vitamin A deficiency)<br>• Lustreless, dry conjunctiva | • **Vitamin A deficiency** (Nutrition)<br>• **Parenchymatous**: Cicatricial degeneration (Trachoma, Chemical burns), | • Vitamin A supplementation (massive dose),<br>• Artificial tears / Lubricants |
| **Subconjunctival Hemorrhage** | • Bright red patch under conjunctiva<br>• Painless, distinct margins | • Trauma, Valsalva (coughing/straining), Hypertension, Bleeding disorders, Acute Hemorrhagic Conjunctivitis, | • Self-resolving (2-3 weeks)<br>• Cold compress initially, then warm<br>• Treat underlying cause (HTN) |

**Key Mnemonics:**
*   **SAFE** Strategy for Trachoma: **S**urgery, **A**ntibiotics, **F**acial cleanliness, **E**nvironmental improvement.
*   **FISTO** for Trachoma signs: **F**ollicles, **I**nfiltration (Papillae), **S**carring (Arlt's line), **T**richiasis, **O**pacity (Corneal).
*   **3Fs** of Trachoma transmission: **F**ingers, **F**lies, **F**omites.
