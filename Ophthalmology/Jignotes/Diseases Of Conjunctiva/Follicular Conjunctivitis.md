# Follicular Conjunctivitis

### Definition and Pathogenesis
**[[Follicular conjunctivitis]]** is a specific type of conjunctival inflammation characterized by the formation of follicles.
*   **Pathology:** [[Follicles]] are localized aggregations of lymphocytes (lymphoid tissue) situated in the subepithelial **[[adenoid layer]]** of the conjunctiva.
*   **Appearance:** They appear as discrete, slightly elevated, translucent, yellowish-white or greyish nodules. They are often described as resembling "boiled sago grains" or "rice grains".
*   **Vascularization:** Unlike [[papillae]], follicles do **not** have a central vascular core. Blood vessels may be seen running around the base or over the surface of the follicle but do not enter it.
*   **Age Factor:** The adenoid layer of the conjunctiva develops 3–4 months post-birth. Therefore, follicular conjunctivitis generally does not occur in **infants younger than 3 months**.

### Clinical Classification
Follicular conjunctivitis is broadly classified based on the duration of symptoms into Acute (< 4 weeks) and Chronic (> 4 weeks).

| **Type** | **Etiology/Condition** | **Key Features** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Acute Follicular Conjunctivitis** | **[[Adenoviral conjunctivitis]]** | Epidemic Keratoconjunctivitis (EKC), Pharyngoconjunctival Fever (PCF). |
| | **[[Enterovirus]]/Coxsackie** | Acute Hemorrhagic Conjunctivitis (Apollo). |
| | **[[Herpes Simplex Virus]] (HSV)** | Primary herpetic infection. |
| | **[[Chlamydia trachomatis]]** | Adult Inclusion Conjunctivitis (Serotypes D-K). |
| | **[[Newcastle disease]]** | Rare, associated with poultry. |
| **Chronic Follicular Conjunctivitis** | **[[Trachoma]]** | Chlamydia trachomatis Serotypes A, B, Ba, C. |
| | **Toxic/Drug-induced** | Molluscum contagiosum, topical medications (e.g., [[Brimonidine]], [[Idoxuridine]], [[Pilocarpine]]). |
| | **[[Parinaud’s oculoglandular syndrome]]** | Unilateral granulomatous conjunctivitis + lymphadenopathy. |
| | **Axenfeld’s/Benign Folliculosis** | Seen in children (school folliculosis), asymptomatic. |

### Acute Follicular Conjunctivitis

#### 1. Adenoviral Conjunctivitis
This is the most common cause of acute follicular conjunctivitis.
*   **Epidemic Keratoconjunctivitis (EKC):**
    *   **Serotypes:** 8, 19, 37.
    *   **Transmission:** Highly contagious; spread by fingers, tonometers, and solutions.
    *   **Clinical Features:** Marked follicular reaction (lower fornix > upper), chemosis, petechial hemorrhages, and tender **[[preauricular lymphadenopathy]]**.
    *   **Corneal Involvement:** Progresses from superficial punctate keratitis (SPK) to **subepithelial infiltrates (nummular opacities)** which may persist for months.
    *   **Membranes:** Pseudomembranes or true membranes may form in severe cases.

*   **Pharyngoconjunctival Fever (PCF):**
    *   **Serotypes:** 3, 7.
    *   **Transmission:** "Swimming pool conjunctivitis" (droplet or waterborne).
    *   **Triad:** Fever + Pharyngitis + Follicular Conjunctivitis.
    *   **Course:** Usually self-limiting.

#### 2. Acute Hemorrhagic Conjunctivitis (Apollo Conjunctivitis)
*   **Causative Agents:** **[[Enterovirus 70]]** and **[[Coxsackie virus]] A24**.
*   **Incubation:** Very short (approx. 24 hours).
*   **Key Sign:** Profuse **[[subconjunctival hemorrhage]]** (starts superotemporally and spreads), lid swelling, and follicles.
*   **Resolution:** Self-limiting in 5–7 days.

#### 3. Adult Inclusion Conjunctivitis
*   **Etiology:** **[[Chlamydia trachomatis]]** Serotypes D–K.
*   **Transmission:** Oculogenital (STD) or swimming pools.
*   **Signs:** Acute onset, large follicles predominating in the **lower fornix** (unlike Trachoma which is upper), superficial punctate keratitis, and micropannus.
*   **Treatment:** Oral [[Azithromycin]] (1g single dose) or Doxycycline.

### Chronic Follicular Conjunctivitis

#### 1. Trachoma
A chronic keratoconjunctivitis caused by **[[Chlamydia trachomatis]]** (Serotypes A, B, Ba, C).
*   **Vector:** Flies (Musca sorbens). Transmission route: **3 F's** (Fingers, Flies, Fomites).
*   **Clinical Stages (WHO - FISTO classification):**
    *   **TF (Trachomatous Inflammation-Follicular):** >5 follicles (>0.5mm) on upper tarsal conjunctiva.
    *   **TI (Trachomatous Inflammation-Intense):** Thickening obscuring >50% of deep tarsal vessels.
    *   **TS (Trachomatous Scarring):** White fibrous bands (Arlt’s line).
    *   **TT (Trachomatous Trichiasis):** At least one eyelash rubbing the globe.
    *   **CO (Corneal Opacity):** Visible opacity over the pupil.
*   **Pathognomonic Signs:**
    *   **[[Arlt’s line]]:** Linear scar in sulcus subtarsalis.
    *   **[[Herbert’s pits]]:** Depressions at the limbus (cicatrized limbal follicles).
    *   **[[Pannus]]:** Vascularization and infiltration of the upper cornea.

#### 2. Toxic/Drug-Induced Conjunctivitis
*   **Mechanism:** Type IV delayed hypersensitivity to topical medications, preservatives (Benzalkonium chloride), or cosmetics.
*   **Common Culprits:** [[Brimonidine]], [[Pilocarpine]], [[Idoxuridine]], [[Neomycin]], Atropine.
*   **Signs:** Follicles usually in the lower fornix and tarsal conjunctiva.
*   **Management:** Discontinuation of the offending agent.

#### 3. Molluscum Contagiosum Conjunctivitis
*   **Etiology:** [[Poxvirus]].
*   **Pathogenesis:** Shedding of viral toxic debris from a lid margin nodule into the conjunctival sac causes a secondary chronic follicular reaction.
*   **Sign:** Umbilicated, waxy, pale nodule on the lid margin.
*   **Treatment:** Excision or cauterization of the lid lesion.

#### 4. Parinaud’s Oculoglandular Syndrome
*   **Definition:** Unilateral granulomatous follicular conjunctivitis + visible preauricular/submandibular lymphadenopathy + Fever.
*   **Etiology:**
    *   **Cat-scratch disease** (*[[Bartonella henselae]]*) - Most common.
    *   **Tularemia** (*Francisella tularensis*).
    *   **Tuberculosis**, **Sporotrichosis**, **Syphilis**.

### Differential Diagnosis: Follicles vs. Papillae

This is a crucial clinical distinction in conjunctival inflammation.

| Feature | **Follicles** | **Papillae** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pathology** | Aggregation of lymphocytes (Lymphoid tissue). | Vascular hyperplasia with inflammatory infiltration. |
| **Appearance** | Greyish-white, translucent, "Sago grain" like. | Reddish, flat-topped, velvety, "Cobblestone" (in Vernal KC). |
| **Vascular Pattern** | Vessels run **around** the base (avascular center). | Central vascular core (glomerulus-like). |
| **Key Associations** | Viral (Adenovirus, HSV), Chlamydia, Toxic. | Allergic (VKC, GPC), Bacterial, Contact lens wear. |
| **Location Preference** | Upper lid (Trachoma), Lower lid (Viral/Inclusion). | Upper tarsal conjunctiva (VKC, GPC). |

### Clinical Implications & Mnemonics

*   **Clinical Pearl:** If you see a "red eye" with follicles and preauricular lymphadenopathy, think **Viral** (Adenovirus) or **Chlamydial**. Bacteria generally cause papillae (except *Moraxella* and *Meningococcus* which can be mixed).
*   **Clinical Pearl:** In neonates (<4 weeks), the presence of "follicles" is impossible due to the lack of an adenoid layer. If suspected, re-evaluate for papillae or other pathology.

*   **Mnemonic for Acute Follicular Conjunctivitis:** **"HINCAP"**
    *   **H**erpes simplex
    *   **I**nclusion conjunctivitis
    *   **N**ewcastle disease
    *   **C**oxsackie (Hemorrhagic)
    *   **A**denovirus (EKC)
    *   **P**haryngoconjunctival Fever

*   **Mnemonic for Trachoma Spread:** **"3 F's"**
    *   **F**ingers
    *   **F**lies
    *   **F**omites.
