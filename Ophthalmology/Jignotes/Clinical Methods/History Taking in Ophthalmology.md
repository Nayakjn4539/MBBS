# [[Clinical Methods]] > History Taking in Ophthalmology

# Clinical Case Proforma: Ophthalmology History Taking

This comprehensive guide outlines the standard proforma for taking an ophthalmic history, detailing specific patient complaints and their associated clinical implications.

## I. Demographic Data

| Data Point | Clinical Implications / What it points towards |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Name** | Patient identification and record keeping. |
| **Age** | **Infants:** [[Congenital cataract]], [[Congenital glaucoma]], [[Retinoblastoma]].<br>**Children/Young Adults:** [[Refractive error]], [[Squint]], [[Keratoconus]].<br>**Middle Age:** [[Presbyopia]], [[Angle-closure glaucoma]].<br>**Elderly (>60):** [[Senile cataract]], [[ARMD]], [[Diabetic retinopathy]]. |
| **Sex** | **Males:** More prone to ocular trauma, [[Color blindness]] (X-linked), [[Eales disease]].<br>**Females:** [[Acute congestive glaucoma]], [[Chronic dacryocystitis]], [[Thyroid eye disease]]. |
| **Occupation** | **Industrial workers/Welders:** Ocular trauma, [[Photo-ophthalmia]], Intraocular foreign bodies.<br>**Computer users:** [[Computer vision syndrome]], [[Asthenopia]].<br>**Farmers:** Fungal [[Corneal ulcer]] (trauma with vegetative matter). |
| **Residence** | Relevant for endemic diseases (e.g., [[Trachoma]] in certain belts, Nutritional deficiency in poor socio-economic areas). |

---

## II. Chief Complaints
List complaints in chronological order. Common complaints include defective vision, redness, pain, watering, discharge, and visual phenomena.

---

## III. History of Present Illness (HPI)

### A. Defective Vision (Diminution of Vision)
Establish the **Onset** (Sudden vs. Gradual), **Progression**, **Duration**, and associated **Pain**.

#### 1. Sudden Painless Loss of Vision
*   **[[Central Retinal Artery Occlusion]] (CRAO):** Sudden "blackout".
*   **Massive [[Vitreous Hemorrhage]]:** Often in diabetics or Eales disease.
*   **[[Retinal Detachment]] (RD):** Involving the macular area (curtain falling sensation).
*   **Ischemic [[Central Retinal Vein Occlusion]] (CRVO).**
*   **[[Optic Neuritis]]:** (Though often associated with pain on eye movement).
*   **Methyl Alcohol Amblyopia.**

#### 2. Sudden Painful Loss of Vision
*   **[[Acute Congestive Glaucoma]]:** Severe pain, nausea, vomiting, colored halos.
*   **[[Acute Iridocyclitis]]:** Photophobia, redness.
*   **Chemical/Mechanical Injuries.**
*   **[[Corneal Ulcer]]:** Especially if central.

#### 3. Gradual Painless Loss of Vision
*   **[[Cataract]]:** Most common cause (Senile).
*   **[[Refractive Errors]]:** Myopia, Hypermetropia, Astigmatism.
*   **[[Primary Open Angle Glaucoma]] (POAG):** "Silent thief of sight".
*   **[[Age-Related Macular Degeneration]] (ARMD).**
*   **[[Diabetic Retinopathy]].**
*   **[[Optic Atrophy]].**

#### 4. Gradual Painful Loss of Vision
*   **Chronic [[Iridocyclitis]].**
*   **Chronic [[Corneal Ulcer]]/Keratitis.**

#### 5. Transient Loss of Vision ([[Amaurosis Fugax]])
*   **Carotid Artery Disease:** Emboli.
*   **[[Papilledema]]:** Transient obscurations lasting seconds (usually on standing up).
*   **Giant Cell Arteritis.**
*   **Migraine.**

#### 6. Night Blindness ([[Nyctalopia]])
*   **Vitamin A Deficiency:** (Xerophthalmia).
*   **[[Retinitis Pigmentosa]]:** Tunnel vision, family history.
*   **Pathological Myopia.**
*   **Peripheral Cortical Cataract.**

#### 7. Day Blindness (Hemeralopia)
*   **Central Opacities:** Central nuclear/polar cataracts or corneal opacity (Vision improves in dim light as pupil dilates).
*   **Congenital Deficiency of Cones.**

---

### B. Visual Phenomena

#### 1. Colored Halos (Rainbows around lights)
*   **[[Acute Congestive Glaucoma]]:** Due to corneal edema. Halos are **not** broken by a stenopaeic slit (Fincham's Test).,
*   **[[Cataract]] (Early/Incipient):** Due to hydration of lens fibers. Halos are **broken** into segments by a stenopaeic slit.
*   **Mucopurulent Conjunctivitis:** Due to mucus on the cornea. Halos disappear after washing the eye.

#### 2. Floaters (Muscae Volitantes)
*   Black spots moving with the eye.
*   **Points to:** [[Vitreous Hemorrhage]], [[Posterior Vitreous Detachment]] (PVD), Vitreous degeneration (Myopia, Senile), or Exudates in vitreous (Uveitis).

#### 3. Flashes of Light (Photopsia)
*   **Points to:** Traction on the retina (Prodromal symptom of [[Retinal Detachment]]), PVD, or Migraine.

#### 4. Diplopia (Double Vision)
*   **Uniocular Diplopia:** Persists even when the other eye is closed. Causes: Subluxated lens, incipient cataract (polyopia), keratoconus, double pupil.,
*   **Binocular Diplopia:** Disappears when one eye is closed. Causes: [[Paralytic Squint]] (nerve palsy), Myasthenia gravis, Blow-out fracture.,

#### 5. Distortion of Vision ([[Metamorphopsia]])
*   Straight lines appear wavy or objects appear smaller (micropsia)/larger (macropsia).
*   **Points to:** Macular pathology like [[Central Serous Chorioretinopathy]] (CSCR) or ARMD.

#### 6. Glare
*   Intolerance to bright light (diffuse scattering).
*   **Points to:** Early cataract, corneal edema, or after refractive surgery.

---

### C. Ocular Discomfort and Appearance

#### 1. Redness (Congestion)
*   **Conjunctival Congestion:** Bright red, more in fornices, moves with conjunctiva, blanches with vasoconstrictors. Points to [[Conjunctivitis]].
*   **Ciliary (Circumcorneal) Congestion:** Dusky red/violaceous, around the limbus, does not move, does not blanch. Points to [[Keratitis]], [[Iridocyclitis]].,
*   **Subconjunctival Hemorrhage:** Bright red patch, homogenous. Points to trauma, hypertension, or bleeding disorders.

#### 2. Pain
*   **Mild/Discomfort:** Conjunctivitis ("gritty" sensation), foreign body sensation.
*   **Moderate/Deep aching:** Iridocyclitis (worse at night).
*   **Severe/Bursting:** Acute Congestive Glaucoma, Endophthalmitis.
*   **Asthenopia:** Eye strain, headache after near work. Points to refractive errors or muscle imbalance.

#### 3. Watering
*   **Epiphora:** Watering due to obstruction of outflow (e.g., Blocked Nasolacrimal Duct, Ectropion).
*   **Lacrimation:** Hypersecretion due to reflex irritation (e.g., Corneal ulcer, foreign body).

#### 4. Discharge
*   **Purulent:** Bacterial corneal ulcer, Gonococcal conjunctivitis.
*   **Mucopurulent:** Bacterial conjunctivitis.
*   **Watery/Serous:** Viral conjunctivitis, allergic conjunctivitis.
*   **Ropy:** [[Spring Catarrh]] (Vernal Keratoconjunctivitis).

---

## IV. History of Past Illness
*   **Trauma:** Can lead to traumatic cataract, retinal detachment, or secondary glaucoma years later.
*   **Ocular Surgery:** History of cataract surgery (risk of [[Aphakia]], [[Pseudophakia]], or [[After Cataract]]).
*   **Similar Episodes:** Recurrent attacks suggest [[Herpes Simplex Keratitis]] or Recurrent [[Uveitis]].
*   **Amblyopia Therapy:** History of patching in childhood.

---

## V. Systemic History
Many systemic diseases have ocular manifestations.
*   **[[Diabetes Mellitus]]:** Diabetic retinopathy, early cataract, rubeosis iridis, refractive changes (myopic shift in hyperglycemia).,
*   **Hypertension:** Hypertensive retinopathy, vascular occlusions (BRVO/CRVO/CRAO).
*   **Thyroid Disorders:** Proptosis, lid retraction, exposure keratopathy.
*   **Tuberculosis/Sarcoidosis:** Granulomatous uveitis.
*   **Rheumatoid Arthritis/Ankylosing Spondylitis:** Uveitis, Scleritis, Dry eye.,
*   **Asthma:** Contraindication for Beta-blocker eye drops (Timolol).

---

## VI. Family History
*   **Glaucoma:** Strong familial tendency in POAG.
*   **Refractive Errors:** High myopia.
*   **Dystrophies:** [[Retinitis Pigmentosa]], Corneal dystrophies.
*   **Congenital Cataract.**
*   **Color Blindness.**

---

## VII. Personal History
*   **Addictions:** Alcohol and Tobacco (Tobacco-alcohol amblyopia, toxic optic neuropathy).
*   **Diet:** Vitamin A deficiency (Night blindness).
*   **Allergies:** Atopy (Associated with Vernal Keratoconjunctivitis, Cataract).
