# Laws Regarding Poisons

The legal framework governing poisons in India is primarily constituted by specific Acts regulating the possession and sale of substances, and the Indian Penal Code (IPC) which deals with the criminal liabilities arising from their misuse.

## 1. The Poisons Act, 1919
This Act was passed to consolidate the laws regulating the importation, possession, and sale of poisons. It repealed the Poisons Act of 1904.

*   **Objective:** To regulate and control the trade of poisons.
*   **Power of State Governments:** The State Government may regulating the possession for sale and the sale of any poison:
    *   Grant of license to possess or sell poisons.
    *   Classes of persons to whom licenses may be granted.
    *   Maximum quantity of poison sold to a person.
    *   Maintenance of a register of sales [[Poison Register]] by the vendor.
    *   Safe custody of poisons and labeling of vessels/packages.
    *   Inspection and examination of poisons by authorized officers.
*   **Power of Central Government:** It regulates the importation of poisons into India.
*   **Penalties:**
    *   **First Conviction:** Imprisonment up to 3 months or fine up to ₹500, or both.
    *   **Subsequent Conviction:** Imprisonment up to 6 months or fine up to ₹1,000, or both.
    *   Any poison in respect of which an offense has been committed is liable to confiscation.

## 2. The Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1940
This Act regulates the import, manufacture, distribution, and sale of drugs and cosmetics.
*   **Schedules:** It classifies drugs into schedules.
    *   **Schedule H & L:** Prescription drugs which cannot be sold without a prescription from a registered medical practitioner.
    *   **Schedule X:** Psychotropic drugs requiring special licenses for manufacture and sale.
*   **Section 26A:** The government has the power to prohibit the manufacture, sale, or distribution of any drug or cosmetic likely to involve risk to humans or animals (e.g., prohibition of amidopyrine).

## 3. The Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances (NDPS) Act, 1985
This is the most critical legislation regarding drugs of abuse. It repealed the Opium Act of 1857, the Opium Act of 1878, and the Dangerous Drugs Act of 1930.

### Objectives
*   To consolidate and amend the law relating to narcotic drugs.
*   To make stringent provisions for the control and regulation of operations relating to [[Narcotic Drugs]] and [[Psychotropic Substances]].
*   To establish the **Narcotic Control Bureau (NCB)**.

### Important Definitions
1.  **Narcotic Drug:** Includes Coca leaf, Cannabis (Hemp), Opium, Poppy straw, and all manufactured drugs.
2.  **Psychotropic Substance:** Any substance, natural or synthetic, or any natural material or any salt or preparation of such substance or material included in the list of psychotropic substances (e.g., LSD, Amphetamines, Barbiturates, Benzodiazepines).
3.  **Addict:** A person addicted to any narcotic drug or psychotropic substance.
4.  **Cannabis (Hemp):**
    *   **Charas:** Separated resin (crude or purified) obtained from the cannabis plant (includes Hashish oil).
    *   **Ganja:** Flowering or fruiting tops of the cannabis plant (excluding seeds and leaves when not accompanied by tops).
    *   **Mixture:** Any mixture of Charas or Ganja.
    *   *Note: Bhang (leaves) does not fall under the definition of Cannabis in this Act unless it is part of a mixture or prepared product.*
5.  **Opium:** Coagulated juice of the opium poppy (*Papaver somniferum*).
6.  **Manufactured Drug:** Includes all coca derivatives, medicinal cannabis, opium derivatives, and poppy straw concentrate.

### Prohibitions
The Act prohibits the following operations regarding any narcotic drug or psychotropic substance:
*   Cultivation
*   Production
*   Manufacture
*   Possession
*   Sale/Purchase
*   Transport
*   Warehousing
*   Use/Consumption
*   Import/Export (inter-state or into/out of India)

### Punishments and Penalties
The severity of punishment depends on the quantity of the drug involved.

| Offense Category | Criteria | Punishment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Small Quantity** | Quantity specified by Govt (e.g., Heroin < 5g) | Rigorous Imprisonment (RI) up to 6 months OR Fine up to ₹10,000 OR both. |
| **Commercial Quantity** | Quantity specified by Govt (e.g., Heroin > 250g) | RI 10 to 20 years AND Fine ₹1 to ₹2 Lakhs. |
| **Intermediate Quantity** | Between Small and Commercial | RI up to 10 years AND Fine up to ₹1 Lakh. |

**Consumption of Drugs (Section 27):**
*   Cocaine, Morphine, Heroin: RI up to 1 year or Fine up to ₹20,000 or both.
*   Other drugs: RI up to 6 months or Fine up to ₹10,000 or both.

**Enhanced Punishment (Section 31A - Death Penalty):**
*   For certain repeat offenses involving large quantities (e.g., Opium > 10 kg, Heroin > 1 kg, Hashish > 20 kg), the court may impose the **death penalty**. Note: Judicial discretion now applies, and it is not mandatory.

**Immunity (Section 64A):**
*   Addicts charged with consumption or offenses involving small quantities can be granted immunity from prosecution if they volunteer for de-addiction treatment in a recognized institution.

## 4. Relevant Sections of Indian Penal Code (IPC)

| Section | Description | Punishment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Section 284 IPC** | **Negligent conduct with respect to poisonous substances.** Handling poison in a rash or negligent manner endangering human life. | Imprisonment up to 6 months, or fine up to ₹1,000, or both. |
| **Section 299 IPC** | **Culpable Homicide.** Causing death by doing an act with the intention of causing death or bodily injury likely to cause death. | Up to Life Imprisonment. |
| **Section 300 IPC** | **Murder.** Deliberate administration of lethal poison implies intention to cause death. | Death or Life Imprisonment. |
| **Section 304A IPC** | **Causing death by negligence.** Death caused by a rash or negligent act not amounting to culpable homicide (e.g., pharmacist leaving toxic drugs unlocked accessible to children). | Imprisonment up to 2 years, or fine, or both. |
| **Section 324 IPC** | Voluntarily causing **hurt** by dangerous weapons or means (includes administering poison/corrosive substance). | Imprisonment up to 3 years, or fine, or both. |
| **Section 326 IPC** | Voluntarily causing **grievous hurt** by dangerous weapons or means (includes poisons/corrosives). | Life imprisonment or imprisonment up to 10 years + Fine. |
| **Section 328 IPC** | Causing hurt by means of **poison** with intent to commit an offense. Administering any stupefying, intoxicating, or unwholesome drug (e.g., Date rape drugs, Datura for robbery). | Imprisonment up to 10 years + Fine. |

### Mnemonic for IPC Sections
*   **284:** **2** **8**ad **4** (Two Bad For) Handling -> Negligent conduct.
*   **328:** **3** **2** **8** (Three To Eat) -> Administering poison (usually in food/drink) to commit a crime.

## 5. Medicolegal Duties of a Doctor
In cases of suspected poisoning, the doctor has specific legal obligations:
1.  **Section 39 CrPC:** Every person (including doctors) aware of the commission of an offense (like homicidal poisoning) must inform the nearest magistrate or police officer. Failure is punishable under **Section 176 IPC**.
2.  **Section 175 CrPC:** A doctor is legally bound to furnish information regarding suspected poisoning to the investigating officer. No professional secrecy applies here.
3.  **Preservation of Evidence:** The doctor must preserve gastric lavage, vomitus, urine, and contaminated clothes/food. Intentional omission to do so is punishable under **Section 201 IPC** (Causing disappearance of evidence).
