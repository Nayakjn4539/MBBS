# Anterior Uveitis (Iridocyclitis)

[[Anterior uveitis]] is the inflammation of the anterior portion of the [[uveal tract]], specifically the [[iris]] (iritis) and the pars plicata of the [[ciliary body]] (cyclitis). As these structures are anatomically contiguous, they are often inflamed simultaneously, termed **iridocyclitis**.

### Classification

**1. Anatomical Classification (SUN Working Group)**
*   **Iritis:** Inflammation predominantly affecting the iris.
*   **Anterior Cyclitis:** Inflammation predominantly affecting the anterior ciliary body.
*   **Iridocyclitis:** Inflammation of both iris and anterior ciliary body.

**2. Clinical Classification**
*   **Acute:** Sudden onset, duration < 3 months.
*   **Chronic:** Insidious onset, duration > 3 months.
*   **Recurrent:** Repeated episodes separated by inactive periods (>3 months without treatment).

**3. Pathological Classification**
*   **Suppurative (Purulent):** Due to pyogenic organisms.
*   **Non-suppurative:**
    *   **Non-granulomatous:** Acute onset, fine KPs, intense aqueous flare.
    *   **Granulomatous:** Chronic onset, mutton-fat KPs, iris nodules.

---

### Etiology and Pathogenesis

The etiology is complex and often idiopathic.
*   **Infectious:**
    *   **Bacterial:** Tuberculosis, Syphilis, Leprosy.
    *   **Viral:** [[Herpes simplex]], [[Herpes zoster]], CMV.
    *   **Parasitic/Fungal:** Rare in anterior uveitis alone.
*   **Immune-Mediated/Systemic Associations:**
    *   **HLA-B27 Associated:** [[Ankylosing spondylitis]], [[Reiter’s syndrome]], Psoriatic arthritis.
    *   **Other Autoimmune:** [[Sarcoidosis]], [[Behcet's disease]], [[Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis]] (JIA).
*   **Traumatic:** Blunt or penetrating trauma.
*   **Idiopathic:** Approximately 50% of cases.

---

### Clinical Features

#### Symptoms
*   **Pain:** Dull, aching, throbbing pain, typically worse at night. It is referred along the branches of the trigeminal nerve (V1) to the forehead and scalp.
*   **Redness:** Due to circumcorneal congestion.
*   **Photophobia:** Due to ciliary spasm and trigeminal irritation.
*   **Lacrimation:** Reflex watering.
*   **Blurred Vision:** Due to corneal haze, aqueous turbidity, pupillary block, or induced myopia (ciliary spasm).

#### Signs (Systematic Examination)

**1. Lid and Adnexa**
*   Mild [[lid edema]] may be present.

**2. Conjunctiva**
*   **[[Circumcorneal congestion]] (Ciliary flush):** Deep, violaceous hue around the limbus due to engorgement of anterior ciliary vessels.

**3. Cornea**
*   **Corneal Edema:** Due to toxic endothelitis or raised IOP.
*   **[[Keratic Precipitates]] (KPs):** Proteinaceous cellular deposits on the corneal endothelium. They are usually arranged in **Arlt's Triangle** (inferior triangular area) due to convection currents.

| Type of KP | Appearance | Association |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Mutton Fat KPs** | Large, greasy, yellowish-white | Granulomatous uveitis (TB, Sarcoidosis) |
| **Small/Medium KPs** | Discrete, dirty white | Non-granulomatous uveitis |
| **Fine/Stellate KPs** | Diffuse distribution ("Endothelial dusting") | [[Fuchs’ heterochromic iridocyclitis]], Viral uveitis |
| **Pigmented KPs** | Dark spots | Old/Healed uveitis |

**4. Anterior Chamber (AC)**
*   **[[Aqueous Flare]]:** Leakage of protein due to breakdown of blood-aqueous barrier. Visible as a beam of light (Tyndall effect). Graded 0 to +4.
*   **[[Aqueous Cells]]:** Lymphocytes/inflammatory cells floating in AC. **Sign of active inflammation.** Graded 0 to +4.
*   **[[Hypopyon]]:** Sterile pus (leukocytes) in the lower AC. Seen in Behcet’s disease (mobile, cold hypopyon) and HLA-B27 uveitis (fibrinous, fixed hypopyon).
*   **[[Hyphaema]]:** Blood in AC. Seen in Herpetic uveitis, Gonococcal uveitis, or trauma.

**5. Iris**
*   **Muddy Iris:** Loss of normal crypts/pattern due to edema (water-logging).
*   **Iris Nodules:** (Feature of Granulomatous Uveitis).
    *   **[[Koeppe nodules]]:** At the pupillary margin (ectodermal).
    *   **[[Busacca nodules]]:** On the iris surface/stroma (mesodermal).
*   **Iris Atrophy:** Sectoral (Herpes Zoster), Diffuse (Fuchs').
*   **Neovascularization:** [[Rubeosis iridis]] in chronic cases (e.g., Fuchs').

**6. Pupil**
*   **Miosis:** Small, constricted pupil due to sphincter irritation and edema.
*   **Irregular Pupil:** Due to [[posterior synechiae]] (adhesions between iris and lens). Upon dilation, it forms a **Festooned Pupil**.
*   **Occlusio Pupillae:** Pupil completely covered by exudates/membrane.
*   **Seclusio Pupillae:** 360° ring synechiae preventing aqueous flow $\rightarrow$ leads to **[[Iris Bombe]]**.

**7. Lens**
*   Pigment dispersal on the anterior capsule.
*   **[[Complicated Cataract]]:** Posterior subcapsular opacity with "bread-crumb" appearance and polychromatic lustre.

**8. Intraocular Pressure (IOP)**
*   **Low:** Usually low due to ciliary shutdown (decreased aqueous secretion).
*   **High:** [[Hypertensive uveitis]] (Trabeculitis/clogging of meshwork) or Secondary Angle Closure (Pupillary block/Synechiae).

---

### Differential Diagnosis

**Granulomatous vs. Non-Granulomatous Uveitis**

| Feature | Non-Granulomatous | Granulomatous |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Onset** | Acute | Insidious/Chronic |
| **Pain/Redness** | Marked | Minimal/Mild |
| **KPs** | Small, fine | Large, **Mutton Fat** |
| **Iris Nodules** | Absent | Present (Koeppe/Busacca) |
| **Posterior Synechiae** | Thin, tenuous | Thick, broad-based |
| **Etiology** | Allergic, HLA-B27, Idiopathic | TB, Sarcoidosis, Syphilis, Sympathetic Ophthalmia |

---

### Complications

1.  **[[Complicated Cataract]]:** Polychromatic lustre, posterior subcapsular.
2.  **Secondary Glaucoma:**
    *   Early: Clogging of trabecular meshwork by inflammatory cells/proteins.
    *   Late: Seclusio pupillae $\rightarrow$ Iris Bombe $\rightarrow$ Angle closure.
3.  **[[Band Shaped Keratopathy]]:** Calcium deposition in Bowman’s membrane (common in chronic uveitis e.g., JIA).
4.  **[[Cystoid Macular Edema]] (CME):** Common cause of visual loss.
5.  **[[Phthisis Bulbi]]:** Atrophic, shrunken, hypotensive eye due to ciliary body disorganization.
6.  **Cyclitic Membrane:** Fibrosis behind the lens.

---

### Investigations

A targeted approach is preferred over "shotgun" investigations.
*   **Hematology:** CBC, ESR (nonspecific inflammation).
*   **Serology:** VDRL/TPHA (Syphilis), ACE levels (Sarcoidosis), HLA-B27 typing (Ankylosing spondylitis), Toxoplasma serology.
*   **Radiology:**
    *   **Chest X-ray:** TB, Sarcoidosis (hilar lymphadenopathy).
    *   **X-ray SI Joints:** Ankylosing Spondylitis (Sacroiliitis).
*   **Skin Tests:** Mantoux (TB), Kveim (Sarcoidosis - obsolete), Pathergy test (Behcet's).
*   **Anterior Chamber Tap:** PCR for viral DNA (HSV, VZV, CMV) in atypical cases.

---

### Treatment

**Goals:** Control inflammation, prevent synechiae, relieve pain, preserve vision.

#### 1. Non-Specific Treatment

**A. Cycloplegics (Mydriatics)**
*   **Drugs:** [[Atropine]] 1% ointment/drops (Drug of Choice), Homatropine 2%, Cyclopentolate.
*   **Mechanism of Action:**
    1.  Relieves ciliary spasm $\rightarrow$ **Relieves Pain**.
    2.  Dilates pupil $\rightarrow$ Prevents/Breaks **[[Posterior Synechiae]]**.
    3.  Reduces vascular permeability $\rightarrow$ Decreases exudation.
    4.  Increases blood supply to anterior uvea $\rightarrow$ More antibodies reach the tissue.

**B. Corticosteroids (Anti-inflammatory)**
*   **Topical:** Prednisolone acetate 1% (Gold standard), Dexamethasone, Betamethasone. Frequent dosing (e.g., hourly) in acute stage, then tapered.
*   **Periocular Injection:** Sub-Tenon triamcinolone (for severe/CME cases).
*   **Systemic:** Oral prednisolone for resistant cases or bilateral severe disease.

**C. NSAIDs:** Topical (Flurbiprofen, Nepafenac) or oral for pain relief and prevention of CME.

**D. Immunosuppressives:** (Methotrexate, Azathioprine, Cyclosporine) for steroid-resistant cases or steroid-sparing therapy.

**E. Biologics:** Infliximab/Adalimumab for severe Behcet’s or HLA-B27 uveitis.

#### 2. Specific Treatment
*   Treat the underlying cause (e.g., ATT for TB, Penicillin for Syphilis, Acyclovir for Herpetic uveitis).

#### 3. Management of Complications
*   **Raised IOP:**
    *   Use: Beta-blockers (Timolol), Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors (Acetazolamide), Alpha-agonists.
    *   **Contraindicated:** [[Pilocarpine]] (increases inflammation and synechiae), Prostaglandin analogues (may cause CME/inflammation).
*   **Cataract:** Surgery performed only when the eye is quiet for >3 months.

### Important Clinical Syndromes

*   **[[Fuchs' Heterochromic Iridocyclitis]] (FHI):**
    *   *Mnemonic:* **No FUCHS** (No posterior synechiae, Floaters, Unilateral, Cataract, Heterochromia, Stellate KPs).
    *   **Amsler's Sign:** Filiform hemorrhage from angle during surgery.
*   **[[Posner-Schlossman Syndrome]] (Glaucomatocyclitic Crisis):**
    *   Recurrent attacks of mild uveitis with **very high IOP** (40-50 mmHg).
    *   Few KPs, open angle, self-limiting.
*   **[[Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis]] (JIA):**
    *   Common in young girls (Pauciarticular type).
    *   "White Uveitis" (asymptomatic, white eye).
    *   High risk of band keratopathy and complicated cataract. Requires screening.
*   **[[Behcet's Disease]]:**
    *   Triad: Oral ulcers + Genital ulcers + Uveitis.
    *   Characteristic **Cold Hypopyon** (mobile, non-fibrinous).
