# [[ENVIRONMENT & HEALTH]] > Sanitation barrier aims at breaking the transmission cycle of faecal borne Diseases

### Sanitation Barrier

**Definition**
The [[Sanitation Barrier]] is the mechanism of segregating human excreta (faeces) from the environment to prevent the spread of diseases. It creates a physical obstacle that breaks the chain of transmission of faecal-borne pathogens to a new host.

**The Problem: Transmission Cycle (The 5 Fs)**
Faecal-borne diseases are transmitted from the excreta of a sick person or carrier to a new host through various channels, commonly known as the **5 Fs** (or sometimes 7 Fs).
*   **F**aeces (The source)
*   **F**luids (Water)
*   **F**ingers
*   **F**lies
*   **F**omites
*   **F**ields (Soil)
*   **F**ood

**The Solution: Mechanism of the Barrier**
The sanitation barrier segregates faeces so that the disease agent cannot reach the new host directly or indirectly. It effectively "blocks" the path of pathogens at the source.

1.  **Segregation of Faeces:** The most effective step is to confine excreta (e.g., in a pit or tank) so it does not contaminate the soil or water.
2.  **Blocking Vectors:** It prevents flies/insects from contacting faeces and then landing on food.
3.  **Preventing Direct Contact:** It stops pathogens from contaminating hands (Fingers) and inanimate objects (Fomites).

**Methods to Establish the Sanitation Barrier**
*   **[[Sanitary Latrine]]:** The primary method. A latrine is considered sanitary if it fulfils specific criteria (does not pollute soil/water, not accessible to flies, no nuisance).
*   **[[Water Seal]]:** In water-seal latrines (e.g., RCA or PRAI type), a small depth of water (approx. 2 cm) in the trap acts as a seal. This prevents:
    *   Access by flies to the night soil.
    *   Escape of foul gases and odours.
*   **Disposal Pits/Septic Tanks:** For safe containment and anaerobic digestion of waste.
*   **Sewerage Systems:** In urban areas, an underground water-carriage system removes excreta to a treatment plant.

**Secondary/Supplementary Measures**
While the physical segregation of faeces is the primary barrier, supplementary measures act as secondary barriers:
*   Protection of water supplies (Chlorination).
*   Personal hygiene (Hand washing).
*   Food sanitation (Covering food, washing vegetables).
*   Control of flies.

**Diseases Prevented (Faecal-Borne Diseases)**
Proper sanitation prevents the transmission of:
*   **Bacterial:** [[Typhoid]], Paratyphoid, [[Cholera]], Bacillary Dysentery.
*   **Viral:** [[Hepatitis A]], Hepatitis E, [[Orthopaedics/Jignotes/Pediatric Orthopaedics/Poliomyelitis]], Rotavirus.
*   **Protozoal:** Amoebiasis, Giardiasis.
*   **Helminthic:** Roundworm (Ascariasis), Hookworm, Whipworm.

**Clinical Relevance**
*   **Sanitation as Medicine:** Improvements in sanitation (breaking the transmission cycle) have a greater impact on reducing community morbidity and mortality from enteric infections than therapeutic interventions alone.
*   **Hookworm Control:** Proper use of sanitary latrines is the primary prevention strategy for Ankylostomiasis by preventing soil pollution with infective larvae.
*   **Epidemic Control:** During outbreaks of Cholera or Hepatitis E, establishing a sanitation barrier (along with water purification) is critical to stop the spread.

**Mnemonic: The 5 Fs**
To remember the routes of transmission blocked by the barrier:
*   **F**lies
*   **F**ingers
*   **F**omites
*   **F**ood
*   **F**luid (Water)
