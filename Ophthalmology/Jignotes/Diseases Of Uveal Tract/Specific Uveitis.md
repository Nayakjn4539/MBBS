# Specific Uveitis Entities

## 1. Behcet’s Disease
A serious, idiopathic, multisystem occlusive [[vasculitis]] characterized by the triad of oral ulcers, genital ulcers, and ocular inflammation.

### Etiology and Associations
*   **Mechanism:** Obliterative vasculitis probably caused by circulating [[immune complexes]].
*   **Demographics:** Typically affects young men (20–30 years). Common in Mediterranean region, Middle East, and Japan.
*   **Genetic Association:** Strongly associated with **[[HLA-B5]]** and **[[HLA-B51]]**.

### Clinical Features
*   **Systemic:**
    *   **Oral aphthous ulcers:** Recurrent and painful (100% cases).
    *   **Genital ulcers:** Scrotal or vulvar ulceration.
    *   **Skin lesions:** [[Erythema nodosum]], thrombophlebitis.
    *   **Pathergy test:** Positive (hypersensitivity to needle prick).
    *   **Joints:** Non-destructive arthritis.

*   **Ocular (Panuveitis):**
    *   **Anterior Segment:** Recurrent, bilateral, acute [[iridocyclitis]].
        *   **Hypopyon:** A characteristic **"Cold Hypopyon"** may be seen (shifts with head position, minimal fibrin, relatively white/quiet eye compared to the amount of hypopyon).
    *   **Posterior Segment:**
        *   **Retinitis:** White necrotic infiltrates of inner retina.
        *   **Vasculitis:** Diffuse vascular leakage, periphlebitis.
        *   **Vitritis** and [[cystoid macular edema]] (CME).
        *   **Optic neuritis** or atrophy.

### Complications
*   Complicated [[cataract]].
*   Secondary [[glaucoma]].
*   [[Phthisis bulbi]].
*   Poor visual prognosis due to macular ischemia or optic atrophy.

### Treatment
*   **Corticosteroids:** Helpful initially but often insufficient for long-term control.
*   **Immunosuppressives:** Essential for sight-threatening disease. Agents include [[Cyclosporine]], [[Azathioprine]], [[Chlorambucil]], and [[Methotrexate]].

## 2. Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada (VKH) Syndrome
An idiopathic multisystem disorder involving melanocyte-containing tissues (uvea, ear, skin, meninges). It is a granulomatous [[panuveitis]].

### Etiology and Associations
*   **Mechanism:** T-cell mediated autoimmune attack against **[[melanocytes]]**.
*   **Demographics:** Common in pigmented races (Asians, Hispanics, African descent). Peak age 20–50 years.
*   **Genetic Association:** **[[HLA-DR4]]**, [[HLA-DR1]], and [[HLA-DW15]].

### Clinical Stages and Features
**1. Prodromal/Neurological Phase:**
*   Meningismus, headache, encephalopathy.
*   **Auditory:** Tinnitus, vertigo, high-frequency deafness.
*   **CSF:** Lymphocytosis (pleocytosis).

**2. Acute Uveitic Phase:**
*   **Anterior:** Chronic granulomatous [[anterior uveitis]] (Mutton fat KPs, iris nodules).
*   **Posterior:** Multifocal [[choroiditis]] leading to **[[exudative retinal detachment]]**.

**3. Convalescent/Chronic Phase (Skin & Fundus changes):**
*   **Cutaneous:** **[[Alopecia]]** (hair loss), **[[Poliosis]]** (whitening of eyelashes/hair), **[[Vitiligo]]** (skin depigmentation).
*   **Sugiura's Sign:** Perilimbal vitiligo.
*   **Fundus:** "Sunset Glow Fundus" (orange-red appearance due to RPE and choroidal depigmentation).
*   **Dalen-Fuchs nodules:** Small yellowish peripheral choroidal granulomas.

### Treatment
*   **Systemic Steroids:** High dose, early administration is the mainstay (e.g., IV Methylprednisolone followed by oral taper).
*   **Immunosuppressives:** Required for steroid-resistant or recurrent cases.

## 3. Sarcoidosis
A chronic multisystem granulomatous disorder characterized by non-caseating [[granuloma]] formation.

### Etiology
*   **Nature:** Idiopathic, likely immune-mediated.
*   **Demographics:** Young adults (20–50 years), females > males, African heritage > Caucasians.
*   **Genetic Association:** HLA-B8, HLA-B1.

### Ocular Manifestations (20–50% of patients)
*   **Anterior Uveitis:**
    *   Granulomatous (Mutton fat [[keratic precipitates]], [[Koeppe nodules]], [[Busacca nodules]]).
    *   Trabecular nodules leading to [[secondary glaucoma]].
*   **Intermediate Uveitis:**
    *   **[[Snowballs]]:** Cotton-ball opacities in the vitreous.
    *   **String of pearls:** Linear vitreous opacities.
*   **Posterior Uveitis:**
    *   **Candle Wax Drippings:** Periphlebitis with intense perivascular exudates.
    *   Choroidal granulomas.
*   **Adnexa:**
    *   Lacrimal gland enlargement (dry eye/[[keratoconjunctivitis sicca]]).
    *   Conjunctival nodules (biopsy site).

### Specific Syndromes associated with Sarcoidosis
| Syndrome | Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **[[Heerfordt's Syndrome]]** (Uveoparotid Fever) | Uveitis + Parotid enlargement + Facial nerve palsy + Fever |
| **Lofgren Syndrome** | Erythema nodosum + Bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy + Uveitis + Arthropathy |
| **Mikulicz's Syndrome** | Bilateral enlargement of lacrimal and salivary glands. |

### Diagnosis
*   **Chest X-ray/CT:** Bilateral Hilar Lymphadenopathy (BHL).
*   **Serum [[Angiotensin Converting Enzyme]] (ACE):** Elevated.
*   **Serum Calcium:** Hypercalcemia.
*   **Biopsy:** Non-caseating granuloma (from conjunctiva, lacrimal gland, or lung).
*   **Anergy:** Negative Mantoux test (commonly).

## 4. Posner-Schlossman Syndrome (Glaucomatocyclitic Crisis)
A condition characterized by recurrent, unilateral attacks of mild anterior uveitis associated with a marked rise in intraocular pressure (IOP).

### Clinical Features
*   **Demographics:** Young adults (20–50 years).
*   **Symptoms:** Unilateral blurring of vision, colored halos (due to corneal edema), mild discomfort (pain is usually absent or mild).
*   **IOP:** Markedly elevated (40–50 mm Hg) during the attack.
*   **Signs:**
    *   **Eye:** Relatively white (minimal congestion).
    *   **Cornea:** Epithelial edema; Fine, non-pigmented **[[keratic precipitates]]** (KPs) usually inferiorly.
    *   **Chamber:** Deep anterior chamber; **No posterior synechiae** (key differentiator).
    *   **Pupil:** Dilated and sluggish (Contrast: In typical anterior uveitis, the pupil is constricted). *Note: Marrow suggests pupil constriction in general uveitis, but Khurana specifies dilated in PSS.*
*   **Genetic Association:** **[[HLA-BW54]]**.

### Management
*   **Medical:**
    *   **Antiglaucoma drugs:** To lower IOP (e.g., Beta-blockers, CAIs). **Avoid Pilocarpine** (can aggravate inflammation).
    *   **Topical Steroids:** Short course to control inflammation.
    *   **NSAIDs:** Indomethacin.
*   **Surgery:** Rarely needed as attacks are self-limiting, but filtration surgery may be needed if glaucomatous damage progresses.

## Comparison of Specific Uveitis Entities

| Feature | Behcet's Disease | Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada | Sarcoidosis | Posner-Schlossman |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Type** | Non-granulomatous Panuveitis | Granulomatous Panuveitis | Granulomatous Panuveitis | Non-granulomatous Anterior Uveitis |
| **Key Sign** | Mobile/Cold **[[Hypopyon]]** | **[[Sunset Glow Fundus]]** | **[[Candle Wax Drippings]]** | High IOP, **Few KPs** |
| **Systemic** | Oral/Genital Ulcers | Vitiligo, Poliosis, Tinnitus | Hilar Lymphadenopathy | None |
| **HLA** | HLA-B51 | HLA-DR4 | HLA-B8 | HLA-BW54 |
| **Etiology** | Vasculitis | Autoimmune (Melanocytes) | Non-caseating Granuloma | Idiopathic/Viral? |

### Mnemonic for Posner-Schlossman Syndrome
**"POSNER"**
*   **P** - Pressure high (40-60 mmHg)
*   **O** - Open angle / One eye (Unilateral)
*   **S** - Steroids (responsive)
*   **N** - No synechiae
*   **E** - Edema of cornea (halos)
*   **R** - Recurrent attacks
