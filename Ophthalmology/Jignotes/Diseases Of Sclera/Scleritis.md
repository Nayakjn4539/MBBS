# Scleritis

**Scleritis** refers to the granulomatous inflammation of the [[sclera]] proper. It is a much more serious disease than episcleritis, capable of causing visual impairment and even loss of the eye if treated inadequately. It typically occurs in elderly patients (40â€“70 years), affecting females more frequently than males.

## Classification
The classification is based on the anatomical site and the nature of the inflammation (Watson and Hayreh Classification).

> [!warning] Diagram Alert
> Classification flowchart of Scleritis branching into Anterior (98%) and Posterior (2%). Anterior divides into Non-necrotizing (Diffuse/Nodular) and Necrotizing (With inflammation/Without inflammation)

### 1. Anterior Scleritis (98%)
*   **Non-necrotizing (85%):**
    *   **Diffuse:** Widespread inflammation involving a quadrant or more.
    *   **Nodular:** One or more hard, purplish, elevated, immovable nodules.
*   **Necrotizing (13%):**
    *   **With Inflammation:** Vaso-occlusive, Granulomatous, or Surgically induced.
    *   **Without Inflammation:** Also known as [[Scleromalacia perforans]].

### 2. Posterior Scleritis (2%)
*   Inflammation involving the sclera behind the equator.

---

## Etiology and Associations
Approximately 50% of scleritis cases are associated with systemic diseases.

*   **Autoimmune/Connective Tissue Disorders:**
    *   [[Rheumatoid arthritis]] (Most common association; 0.5% of RA patients develop scleritis).
    *   [[Wegener's granulomatosis]] (Granulomatosis with polyangiitis).
    *   [[Polyarteritis nodosa]] (PAN).
    *   [[Systemic lupus erythematosus]] (SLE).
    *   [[Ankylosing spondylitis]].
    *   Relapsing Polychondritis.
*   **Infections:**
    *   [[Herpes zoster ophthalmicus]].
    *   Tuberculosis, Syphilis, Leprosy (Granulomatous diseases).
    *   Pseudomonas or fungal infections (often post-surgical).
*   **Metabolic:** Gout, Thyrotoxicosis.
*   **Surgically Induced Scleritis (SIS):** Rare complication following ocular surgeries like cataract extraction or retinal detachment surgery.

---

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
*   **Pain:** The hallmark symptom. It is moderate to severe, deep, and **boring** in character. It often wakes the patient early in the morning and radiates to the jaw, temple, or forehead.
*   **Redness:** Can be localized or diffuse.
*   **Photophobia and Lacrimation:** Usually mild to moderate.
*   **Diminution of Vision:** May occur occasionally.

### Signs per Clinical Type

#### 1. Anterior Non-necrotizing Scleritis
*   **Diffuse:** Involved area is raised and has a **salmon pink to purple** color.
*   **Nodular:** Nodules are firm, tender, and **immovable** (cannot be moved separately from the sclera, unlike episcleral nodules).

#### 2. Anterior Necrotizing Scleritis
*   **With Inflammation:** Acute severe form. Intense localized inflammation with areas of infarction due to vasculitis. The sclera becomes transparent/ectatic, and the dark uveal tissue shines through.
*   **Without Inflammation ([[Scleromalacia perforans]]):**
    *   Typically seen in elderly women with long-standing seropositive [[Rheumatoid arthritis]].
    *   **Painless** and asymptomatic initially.
    *   Yellowish patch of melting sclera develops due to arterial obliteration.
    *   The sclera becomes dead white (sequestrum) and absorbs, leaving thin sclera with exposed uvea.
    *   Spontaneous perforation is rare unless IOP is elevated.

#### 3. Posterior Scleritis
Often misdiagnosed because the eye may look white anteriorly. Features result from inflammation of adjacent structures:
*   Exudative [[retinal detachment]].
*   [[Macular edema]].
*   Disc edema.
*   Proptosis or restricted ocular movements.
*   **Choroidal folds**.

---

## Complications
*   **Cornea:** [[Sclerosing keratitis]] (triangular opacity advancing from limbus), Keratolysis (melting), Peripheral corneal guttering.
*   **Uvea:** Anterior uveitis (common in necrotizing forms).
*   **Lens:** Complicated [[cataract]].
*   **Glaucoma:** Secondary glaucoma due to angle blockage, synechiae, or steroid therapy.
*   **Globe:** Staphyloma formation (ectasia of sclera).

---

## Investigations
A thorough workup is required to rule out systemic associations.

*   **Blood Tests:** Complete hemogram, ESR, C-reactive protein (CRP), Serum uric acid (Gout).
*   **Immunological Survey:** Rheumatoid factor (RF), Antinuclear antibody (ANA), Antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibody (ANCA), LE cells.
*   **Infectious Disease Screening:** VDRL/FTA-ABS (Syphilis), Mantoux test (TB).
*   **Imaging:**
    *   **B-scan Ultrasonography:** Essential for **Posterior Scleritis**. It typically shows the **"T" sign** (fluid in the sub-Tenon's space creating a T-shape with the optic nerve).
    *   CT/MRI to rule out orbital tumors or foreign bodies.

---

## Differential Diagnosis: Episcleritis vs. Scleritis

| Feature | [[Episcleritis]] | [[Scleritis]] |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pathology** | Inflammation of superficial episcleral tissue | Inflammation of sclera proper |
| **Color/Hue** | Bright red/Pinkish | Bluish-red/Violaceous/Purple |
| **Pain** | Mild discomfort/pricking | Severe, deep, boring pain |
| **Palpation** | Nodule may move over sclera | Nodule is immovable/fixed |
| **Phenylephrine Test** (2.5% or 10%) | **Blanching occurs** (Redness disappears) | **No Blanching** (Redness persists) |
| **Course** | Benign, self-limiting | Serious, potential visual loss |

### Clinical Implication
**Phenylephrine Test:** Instilling phenylephrine drops causes constriction of superficial episcleral vessels but not the deep vascular plexus of the sclera. Therefore, blanching indicates episcleritis, while persistence of redness indicates scleritis.

---

## Treatment

### 1. Non-Infectious Scleritis
*   **Non-necrotizing:**
    *   **Oral NSAIDs:** First line. Indomethacin (75 mg BD), Flurbiprofen (300 mg OD), or Ibuprofen.
    *   **Oral Steroids:** If NSAIDs fail. Prednisolone (1 mg/kg) tapered slowly.
*   **Necrotizing:**
    *   **Systemic Steroids:** High doses (oral or IV methylprednisolone pulse therapy) are often required immediately.
    *   **Immunosuppressive Agents:** Cyclophosphamide, Methotrexate, Azathioprine, or Cyclosporine. Used for steroid-resistant cases or steroid-sparing.
    *   **Biologics:** Infliximab or Rituximab in refractory cases.
*   **Surgical:** Scleral patch graft may be needed for extensive scleral melting or risk of perforation.

### 2. Infectious Scleritis
*   Aggressive antimicrobial therapy (topical and systemic) tailored to the organism.
*   Surgical debridement to debulk infected tissue.

> **WARNING:** **Subconjunctival steroid injections** are **CONTRAINDICATED** in scleritis as they may lead to scleral thinning and perforation.
