# Posterior Uveitis (Choroiditis)

### Definition and Overview
**Posterior Uveitis** refers to the inflammation of the [[choroid]] (choroiditis). Due to the close proximity and the dependence of the outer layers of the [[retina]] on the choroid for nutrition, choroidal inflammation almost always involves the overlying retina. Therefore, the resultant lesion is clinically termed **[[chorioretinitis]]** or **[[retinochoroiditis]]**.

*   **Primary site:** Choroid.
*   **Secondary involvement:** Retina, Vitreous, Optic nerve.

### Classification
Posterior uveitis can be classified based on the distribution of lesions:
1.  **Focal:** Single or few circumscribed patches (e.g., [[Toxoplasmosis]]).
2.  **Multifocal:** Multiple discrete lesions scattered over the fundus (e.g., [[Presumed Ocular Histoplasmosis Syndrome]] (POHS), [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada syndrome]]).
3.  **Diffuse (Disseminated):** Widespread inflammation affecting a large area (e.g., [[Sympathetic ophthalmitis]], [[Syphilis]], [[Tuberculosis]]).

---

### Etiology
The causes are diverse and often overlap with systemic diseases.

| Category | Specific Causes |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Infectious** | |
| *Parasitic* | [[Toxoplasmosis]] (Most common worldwide), [[Toxocariasis]], [[Onchocerciasis]] |
| *Bacterial* | [[Tuberculosis]], [[Syphilis]], [[Leprosy]], [[Brucellosis]] |
| *Viral* | [[Cytomegalovirus]] (CMV), [[Herpes Simplex Virus]] (HSV), [[Varicella Zoster Virus]] (VZV) - Acute Retinal Necrosis (ARN), [[Rubella]] |
| *Fungal* | [[Histoplasmosis]], [[Candidiasis]], [[Cryptococcosis]], [[Pneumocystis carinii]] |
| **Non-Infectious / Immunological** | [[Sarcoidosis]], [[Behcet's disease]], [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada syndrome]] (VKH), [[Sympathetic ophthalmitis]] |
| **White Dot Syndromes** | [[Birdshot retinochoroidopathy]], [[Serpiginous choroidopathy]], [[Acute Multifocal Placoid Pigment Epitheliopathy]] (AMPPE) |
| **Masquerade Syndromes** | [[Intraocular lymphoma]] (Large cell lymphoma), [[Retinoblastoma]], [[Choroidal melanoma]] |

**Clinical Implication:** In immunocompromised patients (e.g., AIDS), opportunistic infections like [[CMV retinitis]] and fungal infections are more common.

---

### Clinical Features

#### Symptoms
Posterior uveitis is typically a **painless** condition (unless the anterior segment is involved or secondary glaucoma develops).
*   **[[Floaters]]:** Commonest symptom; due to exudates/cells in the vitreous.
*   **Blurred Vision:** Due to vitreous haze or macular involvement.
*   **[[Photopsia]]:** Flashes of light due to retinal irritation.
*   **[[Metamorphopsia]]:** Distorted vision (straight lines appear wavy) due to macular edema or elevation.
*   **[[Micropsia]] / [[Macropsia]]:** Objects appear smaller (due to separation of photoreceptors) or larger (due to crowding of photoreceptors).
*   **[[Scotoma]]:** A positive scotoma (fixed dark spot) corresponding to the lesion.

#### Signs
1.  **Vitreous Changes:**
    *   **[[Vitritis]]:** Cellular infiltration and flare in the vitreous.
    *   **Vitreous Opacities:** Fine dust-like, coarse, stringy, or "snowball" opacities (aggregates of inflammatory cells).
    *   **Liquefaction:** Syneresis and posterior vitreous detachment (PVD) in chronic cases.

2.  **Fundus Lesions (Choroiditis Patches):**
    *   **Active Lesion:** Pale yellow or dirty white raised area with ill-defined fluffy edges. The overlying retina is cloudy/edematous. Retinal vessels passing over it may be obscured.
    *   **Healed Lesion:** Sharply defined, white area (due to sclera shining through atrophic choroid). Pigmented clumps are seen at the periphery (heaped up pigment).

3.  **Vascular Changes:**
    *   **[[Perivasculitis]] / [[Sheathing]]:** White cuffing around blood vessels.
        *   *Candle wax drippings:* Thick perivascular exudates seen in [[Sarcoidosis]].

### Specific Clinical Entities

#### 1. Toxoplasmosis
*   **Cause:** *Toxoplasma gondii*.
*   **Nature:** Necrotizing retinochoroiditis.
*   **Classic Sign:** **"Headlight in Fog" appearance**.
    *   Focal, white, fluffy necrotizing lesion (Headlight).
    *   Seen through intense vitreous haze/vitritis (Fog).
*   **Recurrence:** Often occurs at the edge of an old healed pigmented scar (Satellite lesion).

#### 2. Sarcoidosis
*   **Type:** Granulomatous uveitis.
*   **Fundus Signs:**
    *   **Candle wax drippings:** Periphlebitis with exudates.
    *   Choroidal granulomas.
    *   String of pearls in vitreous.

#### 3. Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Retinitis
*   **Patient:** Immunocompromised (CD4 count < 50 in HIV).
*   **Fundus Appearance:** **"Pizza Pie"** or **"Cheese and Ketchup"** appearance.
    *   Cheese: White areas of retinal necrosis.
    *   Ketchup: Large superficial hemorrhages.

#### 4. Syphilis
*   **Appearance:** "Salt and Pepper fundus" (also seen in Rubella and Leber's congenital amaurosis).
*   **Forms:** Multifocal chorioretinitis, Neuroretinitis.

---

### Complications
1.  **[[Cystoid Macular Edema]] (CME):** Most common cause of visual loss.
2.  **[[Complicated Cataract]]:** Posterior subcapsular type.
3.  **[[Secondary Glaucoma]]:** Due to pupillary block or trabecular damage.
4.  **[[Retinal Detachment]]:**
    *   Exudative RD (due to fluid accumulation).
    *   Tractional RD (due to vitreous bands).
5.  **[[Choroidal Neovascularization]] (CNV):** Neovascular membrane formation leading to hemorrhage.
6.  **[[Optic Neuritis]] / [[Papillitis]]:** Inflammation extending to the disc.
7.  **[[Phthisis Bulbi]]:** Atrophic shrinkage of the globe in end-stage disease.

---

### Investigations

#### Ocular Imaging
1.  **[[Fundus Fluorescein Angiography]] (FFA):**
    *   *Active lesion:* Early hypofluorescence (masking by edema) followed by late hyperfluorescence (leakage of dye).
    *   *Healed lesion:* Window defect (hyperfluorescence due to RPE atrophy) with no leakage.
    *   Essential to detect CME and CNVM.
2.  **[[Indocyanine Green Angiography]] (ICGA):** Better visualization of choroidal vasculature and inflammatory lesions than FFA.
3.  **[[Optical Coherence Tomography]] (OCT):** Gold standard for detecting and monitoring [[Cystoid Macular Edema]] and subretinal fluid.
4.  **[[Ultrasonography]] (B-Scan):** Useful when media is hazy (e.g., dense vitritis or cataract) to rule out Retinal Detachment or tumors.

#### Systemic Investigations
*   **Blood:** CBC, ESR (nonspecific inflammation).
*   **Serology:**
    *   VDRL/TPHA (Syphilis).
    *   Toxoplasma IgG/IgM.
    *   HIV ELISA.
    *   ACE levels (Sarcoidosis).
*   **Radiology:** Chest X-ray (TB, Sarcoidosis), X-ray Sacroiliac joints (Ankylosing Spondylitis).
*   **Skin Tests:** Mantoux (TB), Kveim (Sarcoidosis - less common now).

### Treatment

#### 1. Nonspecific Therapy
Aimed at controlling inflammation and preserving vision.
*   **Systemic Corticosteroids:** Mainstay for posterior uveitis. Oral Prednisolone or IV Methylprednisolone (pulse therapy) for severe cases.
*   **Periocular Steroids:** Posterior Sub-Tenon (PST) injection (Triamcinolone) for unilateral intermediate or posterior uveitis.
    *   *Contraindication:* Infectious uveitis (e.g., Toxoplasmosis) without specific antimicrobial cover.
*   **[[Immunosuppressives]]:** Used for steroid-resistant cases or steroid-sparing therapy (e.g., Methotrexate, Azathioprine, Cyclosporine).
*   **Biologics:** Anti-TNF agents (e.g., Infliximab, Adalimumab) for refractory cases like Behcet's.

#### 2. Specific Therapy
Targeted at the causative organism.
*   **Toxoplasmosis:** Triple therapy (Pyrimethamine + Sulfadiazine + Folinic acid). Alternative: Clindamycin or Co-trimoxazole. Systemic steroids are added *only* under antibiotic cover to reduce collateral damage.
*   **Tuberculosis:** Anti-tubercular therapy (ATT).
*   **Syphilis:** Penicillin G.
*   **Viral (CMV/HSV):** Systemic/Intravitreal Ganciclovir, Foscarnet, or Acyclovir/Valacyclovir.

#### 3. Surgical Management
*   **[[Vitrectomy]] (Pars Plana Vitrectomy):** Indicated for:
    *   Diagnostic biopsy (in diagnostic dilemmas or lymphoma).
    *   Clearing non-resolving vitreous hemorrhage/opacities.
    *   Relieving tractional retinal detachment.
