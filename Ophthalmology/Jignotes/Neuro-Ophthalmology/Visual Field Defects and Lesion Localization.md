# Visual Field Defects and Lesion Localization

> [!warning] Diagram Alert
> Visual Pathway diagram showing the retina, optic nerve, chiasm, tract, LGN, radiations, and cortex with corresponding field cuts

The localization of lesions within the [[visual pathway]] relies on the specific pattern of visual field loss (scotomas, hemianopias, or quadrantanopias). The anatomy of the nerve fibers determines the defect.

### 1. General Anatomical Principles
*   **Retinal Image:** The image on the retina is inverted and reversed.
    *   **Temporal Retina:** Sees the **Nasal** visual field.
    *   **Nasal Retina:** Sees the **Temporal** visual field.
*   **Pre-Chiasmal (Optic Nerve):** Lesions cause ipsilateral monocular vision loss.
*   **Chiasmal:** Lesions involving crossing fibers cause heteronymous defects (usually bitemporal).
*   **Post-Chiasmal (Retrochiasmal):** Lesions cause contralateral homonymous defects.

### 2. Optic Nerve Lesions
Lesions here affect only one eye (unless bilateral).
*   **Pathology:** [[Optic Neuritis]], Trauma, [[Ischemic Optic Neuropathy]].
*   **Visual Field Defect (VFD):**
    *   **Ipsilateral Blindness:** Complete transection leads to total loss of vision in that eye.
    *   **Central Scotoma:** Loss of central vision. This is the earliest defect in optic neuritis.
    *   **Centrocaecal Scotoma:** Defect extending from the fixation point to the blind spot (seen in [[Toxic Amblyopia]]).

### 3. Optic Chiasm Lesions
The [[Optic Chiasm]] is where the nasal retinal fibers (temporal visual field) decussate (cross over).

**A. Central Chiasmal Lesion**
*   **Mechanism:** Compression of crossing nasal fibers.
*   **VFD:** **Bitemporal Hemianopia** (Heteronymous).
    *   Initially may present as Bitemporal Superior Quadrantanopia (compression from below) or Bitemporal Inferior Quadrantanopia (compression from above).
*   **Causes:**
    *   **Compression from below:** [[Pituitary Adenoma]] (Most common).
    *   **Compression from above:** [[Craniopharyngioma]], 3rd Ventricle Glioma.
    *   **Anterior Communicating Artery Aneurysm**.

**B. Lateral Chiasmal Lesion**
*   **Mechanism:** Compression of uncrossed temporal fibers on the lateral aspects.
*   **VFD:** **Binasal Hemianopia**.
*   **Causes:** Distension of the 3rd ventricle, Atheroma of carotids (Internal Carotid Artery aneurysms causing lateral compression).

**C. Anterior Chiasm (Junction of Optic Nerve and Chiasm)**
*   **Anatomy:** The **Anterior Knee of Wilbrand** contains inferonasal fibers from the contralateral eye that loop forward into the proximal optic nerve before crossing.
*   **VFD:** **Junctional Scotoma**.
    *   Ipsilateral central scotoma (optic nerve compression).
    *   Contralateral superotemporal quadrant defect (damage to the knee of Wilbrand).
*   **Cause:** [[Meningioma]].

### 4. Optic Tract Lesions
The [[Optic Tract]] contains ipsilateral temporal fibers and contralateral nasal fibers.
*   **VFD:** **Contralateral Homonymous Hemianopia**.
*   **Characteristics:**
    *   **Incongruous:** The defects in the two eyes do not match perfectly in shape or size.
    *   **Wernicke's Hemianopic Pupil:** Light reflex is absent when light is shone on the hemianopic retina (blind side) but present on the normal side. This is difficult to elicit clinically.
    *   **Optic Atrophy:** Descending type (band atrophy) may develop.

### 5. Lateral Geniculate Body (LGN) Lesions
*   **VFD:**
    *   **Contralateral Homonymous Hemianopia**.
    *   **Keyhole Defect:** Incomplete lesions may produce a sectoranopia or keyhole shaped defect.
*   **Pupil:** Normal pupillary reflexes (pupillary fibers exit before LGN).

### 6. Optic Radiations (Geniculocalcarine Tract)
The fibers fan out into the parietal and temporal lobes.
*   **VFD:** Contralateral Homonymous Hemianopia (or Quadrantanopia).
*   **Characteristics:**
    *   **Congruity:** Defects are generally **incongruous** in anterior radiation lesions and become more **congruous** (matching) as lesions move posteriorly toward the cortex.
    *   **Pupil:** Normal pupillary reflexes.
    *   **Optic Disc:** No optic atrophy (trans-synaptic degeneration is rare).

**A. Temporal Lobe Lesions (Meyer’s Loop)**
*   **Anatomy:** Inferior fibers loop around the lateral ventricle in the temporal lobe.
*   **VFD:** **Contralateral Homonymous Superior Quadrantanopia**.
*   **Mnemonic:** **"Pie in the Sky"**.
*   **Associated signs:** Temporal lobe seizures, hallucinations, uncinate fits.

**B. Parietal Lobe Lesions (Baum’s Loop)**
*   **Anatomy:** Superior fibers pass through the parietal lobe.
*   **VFD:** **Contralateral Homonymous Inferior Quadrantanopia**.
*   **Mnemonic:** **"Pie on the Floor"**.
*   **Associated signs:** Gerstmann syndrome (acalculia, agraphia, finger agnosia, left-right disorientation).

| Location | Visual Field Defect | Congruity | Pupil Reflex | Mnemonic |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Temporal Lobe** | Superior Quadrantanopia | Incongruous | Normal | Pie in the Sky |
| **Parietal Lobe** | Inferior Quadrantanopia | Incongruous | Normal | Pie on the Floor |

### 7. Visual Cortex (Occipital Lobe) Lesions
The [[Visual Cortex]] (Area 17) is located in the occipital lobe around the calcarine fissure.
*   **VFD:** **Contralateral Homonymous Hemianopia**.
*   **Characteristics:**
    *   **Congruous:** Defects are perfectly symmetrical in both eyes.
    *   **Macular Sparing:** Common in vascular occlusions (PCA stroke). The macula is spared because the occipital pole has dual blood supply from the **Posterior Cerebral Artery (PCA)** and the **Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA)**.
    *   **Macular Splitting:** Occurs if the lesion involves the tip of the occipital cortex (macular representation).
    *   **Temporal Crescent Sparing:** A unilateral lesion may spare the extreme temporal peripheral field of the contralateral eye (represented monocularly in the anterior calcarine cortex).

**Specific Occipital Defects:**
1.  **Tip of Cortex Lesion:** Contralateral Homonymous Macular Defect (Cookie center defect).
2.  **Bilateral Occipital Lesions:** [[Cortical Blindness]] (Anton’s Syndrome - denial of blindness) with normal pupillary reflexes.

### Summary of Lesion Localization

| Site of Lesion | Visual Field Defect | Key Features/Associations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Optic Nerve** | Ipsilateral Blindness / Central Scotoma | RAPD present; Optic Neuritis |
| **Proximal Optic Nerve** | Junctional Scotoma | Ipsilateral central scotoma + Contralateral superotemporal cut |
| **Optic Chiasm (Central)** | Bitemporal Hemianopia | Pituitary Adenoma (compressing from below) |
| **Optic Tract** | Contralateral Homonymous Hemianopia | Incongruous; Wernicke’s Pupil; Optic Atrophy |
| **Temporal Lobe** | Superior Quadrantanopia | "Pie in the Sky" |
| **Parietal Lobe** | Inferior Quadrantanopia | "Pie on the Floor" |
| **Occipital Cortex** | Contralateral Homonymous Hemianopia | Congruous; **Macular Sparing** (PCA occlusion) |

### Mnemonics for Exam
*   **TIPS:** **T**emporal - **I**nferior (fibers) -> **P**ie in the **S**ky (defect). *(Note: Temporal lobe lesions affect inferior fibers causing superior defects)*.
*   **PITS:** **P**arietal - **I**nferior (defect) - **T**emporal - **S**uperior (defect).
*   **3PC & 4 AC:** Causes of Bitemporal Hemianopia: **3**rd Ventricle glioma, **P**ituitary adenoma, **C**raniopharyngioma, **4**th (Anterior) **C**ommunicating Artery aneurysm.
